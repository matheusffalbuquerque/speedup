## 🖥️ Technology & Communication
### *Tecnologia e Comunicação*

**Level:** ROOT — Lesson 07 of 12
**Duration:** 90 minutes

---

### 🎯 Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:

1. **Recognize and use** core technology vocabulary (nouns and verbs) in natural sentences
2. **Use technology verbs** correctly with their proper structures (e.g., *connect to*, *search for*, *log in/out of*)
3. **Form and understand** the Present Perfect tense in affirmative, negative, and question forms
4. **Use the modal "can"** to talk about tech abilities and limitations
5. **Understand basic norms** of digital communication in English-speaking contexts (email, messaging, social media)

---

> 📌 **Teacher:** Briefly go over the objectives at the start of the lesson. You don't need to explain each point in depth — just give the student a roadmap of where you're going. You might say: *"Today we're going to talk about technology — computers, phones, apps. We'll also learn a very important new grammar structure called the Present Perfect. It's used a lot in English, so let's take it step by step."*

# Slide 2: Warm-Up — Tech Charades

---

## 🎭 Warm-Up: Tech Charades
**Duration:** 5–8 minutes
**Format:** INDIVIDUAL — one student mimes, teacher guesses and confirms
**Goal:** Activate prior knowledge of technology-related actions; get the student moving and engaged before the lesson begins

---

### 📋 How to Run This Activity (Teacher Runbook)

**Step 1 — Set the scene (1 min)**
> 📌 **Teacher:** Say to the student: *"Let's start with a fun activity! You're going to act out some things we do with technology — no talking, no sounds! I'll try to guess what you're doing. Ready?"*

**Step 2 — Demonstrate one action (1 min)**
> 📌 **Teacher:** Mime one action yourself first so the student understands the format. For example, mime typing on a keyboard and say: *"I'm typing on my computer! That's the idea — now it's your turn."*

**Step 3 — Student mimes, teacher guesses (3–5 min)**
> 📌 **Teacher:** Read one action from the list below (or hold up a card if you've prepared them). The student acts it out silently. You guess out loud using English: *"Are you downloading something? Are you sending a message?"* When you guess correctly, say the verb clearly and confirm: *"Yes! You were 'downloading'! Great."*

**Step 4 — Debrief and connect to the lesson (1–2 min)**
> 📌 **Teacher:** After 4–6 mimes, say: *"All of these actions — download, send, connect, share — are technology verbs. Today we're going to learn these words and more. We'll also learn how to talk about things you have or haven't done with technology."*

---

### 🎬 Charades Action List (Student Acts Out)

| # | Action to Mime | Target Verb / Word |
|---|---------------|--------------------|
| 1 | Typing quickly on a keyboard | **type / use a computer** |
| 2 | Looking at a phone and pressing download | **download** |
| 3 | Taking a photo and sending it | **upload / share** |
| 4 | Typing a message and pressing send | **send a message** |
| 5 | Scrolling through a social media feed | **scroll / social media** |
| 6 | Plugging in a charger | **charge / charger** |
| 7 | Searching on Google (typing in a search bar) | **search** |

| 12 | Installing a new app on a phone | **install** |

---

### 💡 Tips for the Teacher

- Keep the energy light and playful — this is meant to reduce anxiety before grammar content
- If the student is shy or uncertain, offer hints: *"Think about your phone..."*
- Celebrate every correct mime enthusiastically: *"Perfect! Exactly right!"*
- You can reverse roles briefly (teacher mimes 1–2 actions) to keep it dynamic in a 1-on-1 context
- The charades list intentionally includes words from today's lesson — the student may not know all of them yet, and that's fine

---

### 📝 Usage Notes

- **Email** is both a noun and a verb: *"Send me an email."* / *"I'll email you."*
- **Phone** in casual English almost always means *mobile phone* (celular), not landline
- **Social media** is grammatically **uncountable** in English: ✅ *"Social media is powerful."* ❌ *"Social medias are..."*
- **App** is short for *application*. Both are acceptable, but *app* is far more common in everyday speech
- **Message** can be used as a verb too: *"I'll message you later."* (muito comum no inglês informal)
- **Website** and **site** are interchangeable in everyday English

---

> - *"Do you check your email every day?"*
> - *"How many hours do you spend on social media?"*
>
> These aren't grammar exercises — just warm conversation to anchor the vocabulary in real experience. Keep it natural and brief (1–2 exchanges per word).

---

### 📝 Teacher Notes — Word by Word

**PASSWORD /ˈpæswɜːrd/**
- The "a" sound is like in "cat" — /pæs/
- Very common in everyday digital life
- Common phrase: *"Forgot your password?"* (seen on every login page)
- Also used figuratively: *"Success is the password to happiness."*

**SCREEN /skriːn/**
- Simple and very practical word
- Notice the cluster /skr/ at the beginning — common error is to omit the /k/
- Related words: *touchscreen, full screen, screenshot*
- Common expression: *"Don't stare at the screen for too long."*

**CHARGER /ˈtʃɑːrdʒər/**
- The /tʃ/ sound is like "ch" in "cheese" — students sometimes say /sh/ instead
- In English: *"My phone is dead"* = bateria acabou / sem bateria
- Common phrase: *"Do you have a charger?"* / *"I forgot my charger."*

**WI-FI /ˈwaɪfaɪ/**
- Pronounced exactly like Portuguese — no adaptation needed
- Common collocations: *Wi-Fi password, Wi-Fi network, public Wi-Fi, connect to Wi-Fi*
- Common sentence: *"Is there Wi-Fi here?"* / *"The Wi-Fi is not working."*

**NOTIFICATION /ˌnoʊtɪfɪˈkeɪʃən/**
- Longer word — stress on the 4th syllable: no-ti-fi-**CA**-tion
- Plural is very common: *"I have 50 notifications."*
- Common collocations: *turn off notifications, push notification, notification settings*
- The verb form: *"The app will notify you."*

**PROFILE /ˈproʊfaɪl/**
- Stress on 1st syllable: **PRO**-file
- Common in social media and professional contexts
- Common expressions: *"Update your profile."* / *"Check their profile."* / *"Profile picture"* (also: *"profile pic"* informally)

---

> 📌 **Teacher:** These expansion words come up naturally in the model dialogues later. You don't need to drill all six heavily — focus on the ones most relevant to the student's digital habits. A good way to introduce them is to ask: *"Do you have notifications turned on for all your apps?"* or *"What's the Wi-Fi network called at home?"* (rhetorical/fun — don't push for personal info). This makes the words feel real and immediately useful.

## ⚡ Technology Verbs
**13 verbs for talking about what we do with technology**

---

### Technology Verbs Table

| Verb | Português | Structure | Example |
|------|-----------|-----------|---------|
| **Download** | Baixar | download + something | "I download movies to watch offline." |
| **Upload** | Enviar / fazer upload | upload + something | "She uploaded her photos to Instagram." |
| **Send** | Enviar | send + something | "I sent you an email this morning." |
| **Receive** | Receber | receive + something | "Did you receive my message?" |
| **Connect** | Conectar | connect + **to** + something | "I can't connect **to** the internet." |
| **Search** | Pesquisar | search + **for** + something | "Let me search **for** the address online." |
| **Share** | Compartilhar | share + something | "She shared a video on her profile." |
| **Post** | Publicar / postar | post + something | "He posts photos every day." |
| **Stream** | Transmitir / assistir online | stream + something | "I stream music while I work." |
| **Follow** | Seguir | follow + someone | "Do you follow any English accounts?" |
| **Log in / Log out** | Entrar / sair de uma conta | log in/out + **of** + something | "Log out **of** your account when you finish." |
| **Install** | Instalar | install + something | "Install the app and create an account." |
| **Update** | Atualizar | update + something | "I need to update my phone." |

---

### 🔗 Key Structural Patterns

**Pattern 1 — Verb + something (direct object)**
Most technology verbs take a direct object with no preposition:
- *"I'll **download** the file."*
- *"Can you **share** this post?"*
- *"She **installed** three apps."*
- *"He **streamed** the match last night."*

**Pattern 2 — Verb + FOR + something**
Used when you're searching or looking:
- *"**Search for** the restaurant name."*
- *"I'm **searching for** a good app."*
> ⚠️ Common error: ❌ *"Search the address."* → ✅ *"Search **for** the address."*

**Pattern 3 — Verb + TO + something**
Used with *connect* and *log in/out*:
- *"**Connect to** the Wi-Fi."*
- *"**Log in to** your account."* (also written: *log into*)
- *"**Log out of** your account."*
> ⚠️ Common error: ❌ *"Connect the internet."* → ✅ *"Connect **to** the internet."*

---

### 📣 Bonus Sentences — Verbs in Natural Context

1. *"I always **download** podcasts before a long trip so I can listen offline."*
2. *"She **uploaded** a video to YouTube and it got 10,000 views in one day."*
3. *"Don't forget to **log out of** your email when you use a public computer."*
4. *"I need to **update** the app — it keeps crashing because it's an old version."*

---

### ⚠️ Common Errors with Technology Verbs

| ❌ Incorrect | ✅ Correct | Explanation |
|-------------|----------|-------------|
| "Download **the** music" | "Download **music**" / "Download **the** song" | *Music* is uncountable — no article. *Song/file* = countable, takes "the" |
| "Connect the internet" | "Connect **to** the internet" | Always use **to** with *connect* |
| "Search the answer" | "Search **for** the answer" | Always use **for** with *search* |
| "I sended you an email" | "I **sent** you an email" | *Send → sent* — irregular past tense |
| "Log out your account" | "Log out **of** your account" | Phrasal verb requires **of** |

---

> 📌 **Teacher:** Go through the verb table and read each example sentence aloud. Ask the student to repeat. Then make observations like: *"Notice that 'connect' always needs 'to' — like 'connect to the internet', 'connect to Wi-Fi'. Let's say it together."* You can also briefly ask: *"Which of these verbs do you use every day?"* — this creates personal connection and aids memory. Point out the irregular past tense of *send → sent* since it appears in the model dialogues.

# Slide 6: Grammar — Present Perfect Introduction

---

## 🟢 Grammar — Present Perfect (Introduction)
**How to talk about experiences and recent events**

---

### What is the Present Perfect?

The **Present Perfect** connects the **past** to the **present**.
We use it when:
- We talk about **life experiences** (without saying when): *"I have used this app."*
- Something **just happened** and is relevant now: *"I've just received your email."*
- We say something **hasn't happened yet** but we expect it to: *"I haven't tried that app yet."*

> 🇧🇷 **Portuguese note:** O Present Perfect não existe em português da mesma forma. A tradução mais comum é com o **pretérito perfeito** ("Eu já usei esse aplicativo.") ou **pretérito perfeito composto** ("Eu tenho usado..."), mas o uso em inglês é diferente. Por enquanto, foque na **forma** e nos **marcadores de tempo** (ever, never, already, yet, just).

---

### 📐 Formula

```
Subject  +  have / has  +  past participle
```

| Subject | Auxiliary | Example |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| I | **have** | I **have downloaded** the app. |
| You | **have** | You **have received** a message. |
| We | **have** | We **have connected** to the Wi-Fi. |
| They | **have** | They **have updated** the system. |
| He | **has** | He **has sent** the email. |
| She | **has** | She **has shared** the photo. |
| It | **has** | It **has updated** automatically. |

---

### 🔗 Contractions Table

| Full Form | Contraction | Example |
|-----------|------------|---------|
| I have | **I've** | I've installed the app. |
| You have | **You've** | You've received a notification. |
| We have | **We've** | We've downloaded the update. |
| They have | **They've** | They've posted a new video. |
| He has | **He's** | He's sent you a message. |
| She has | **She's** | She's updated her profile. |
| It has | **It's** | It's crashed again. |

> ⚠️ Note: *He's / She's / It's* can mean **has** (Present Perfect) OR **is** (Present Simple). Context makes it clear:
> - *"She's updated her profile."* → She **has** updated (Present Perfect)
> - *"She's a developer."* → She **is** a developer (Present Simple)

---

### ✅ Affirmative Examples (with lesson vocabulary)

1. *"I **have used** this app before."*
2. *"She **has received** your message."*
3. *"They **have downloaded** the latest update."*
4. *"He **has followed** me on Instagram."*
5. *"We **have already installed** the software."*

**With contractions (natural speech):**
1. *"I**'ve** used this app before."*
2. *"She**'s** received your message."*
3. *"They**'ve** downloaded the update."*

---

**Regular verbs** (add -ed):

| Base Form | Past Participle |

---

> 📌 **Teacher:** Introduce the Present Perfect gently. Say something like: *"This is a new tense, but don't worry — we'll take it slowly. The most important thing right now is to recognize it and understand what it means. Production comes with practice over time."*
>
> A good anchor: *"Think about your experience with English. Have you ever used an English app? Tell me — not when, just if."* This immediately shows the student the PURPOSE of the tense before drilling the form.
>
> When presenting the contractions, speak them naturally so the student hears *"I've"*, *"She's"* as single sounds — not two separate words.

# Slide 7: Grammar — Present Perfect Negative & Questions

---

## 🔴 Grammar — Present Perfect: Negative & Questions

---

### ❌ Negative Form

**Formula:**
```
Subject  +  haven't / hasn't  +  past participle
```

| Subject | Auxiliary | Full Form | Contraction |
|---------|-----------|-----------|------------|
| I / You / We / They | have not | I **have not** downloaded it | I **haven't** downloaded it |
| He / She / It | has not | She **has not** connected | She **hasn't** connected |

**Negative Examples (with lesson vocabulary):**

1. *"I **haven't downloaded** the update yet."*
2. *"He **hasn't connected** to the internet."*
3. *"We **haven't tried** this app yet."*
4. *"She **hasn't received** any notifications today."*

---

### ❓ Question Form

**Formula:**
```
Have / Has  +  subject  +  past participle  + ?
```

| Auxiliary | Subject | Past Participle | Example |
|-----------|---------|-----------------|---------|
| **Have** | you | used | **Have you used** Instagram? |
| **Have** | they | installed | **Have they installed** the update? |
| **Has** | he | sent | **Has he sent** the message? |
| **Has** | she | connected | **Has she connected** to Wi-Fi? |

**Question Examples (with lesson vocabulary):**

1. *"**Have you ever** used Duolingo?"*
2. *"**Has he ever** sent an email in English?"*
3. *"**Have you** received my message?"*
4. *"**Has she** updated her profile recently?"*

---

### ↩️ Short Answers Table

| Question | Positive Short Answer | Negative Short Answer |
|----------|-----------------------|-----------------------|
| Have **you** done it? | Yes, **I have**. | No, **I haven't**. |
| Have **they** done it? | Yes, **they have**. | No, **they haven't**. |
| Has **he** done it? | Yes, **he has**. | No, **he hasn't**. |
| Has **she** done it? | Yes, **she has**. | No, **she hasn't**. |

> ⚠️ **Important:** In short answers, we **do NOT** use contractions in the positive form:
> - ✅ *"Yes, I **have**."*
> - ✅ *"Yes, she **has**."*
> - ✅ *"No, I **haven't**."* (contraction is fine in negative)

---

### ⚠️ Common Error Corrections

| ❌ Incorrect | ✅ Correct | Rule |
|-------------|----------|------|
| "Have you ever used?" | "Have you ever **used** it?" / "Have you ever **tried** this app?" | Questions usually have an object or context |
| "Yes, I've." | "Yes, I **have**." | No contraction in positive short answers |
| "She haven't sent it." | "She **hasn't** sent it." | *She* takes *has not / hasn't* |
| "Have he received it?" | "**Has** he received it?" | *He* takes *Has* in questions |
| "I have sent it yesterday." | "I **sent** it yesterday." | Use Simple Past with specific time words |

---

> 📌 **Teacher:** The most important drill here is **short answers**. Students often either answer with a full sentence or forget to invert *has/have*. A simple spoken drill works well:
>
> Ask rapid-fire questions using lesson vocabulary and have the student answer with short answers only:
> - *"Have you ever used Netflix?"* → *"Yes, I have."* / *"No, I haven't."*
> - *"Have you downloaded any apps today?"* → *"Yes, I have."*
> - *"Has your phone ever crashed?"* → *"Yes, it has."*
>
> Keep it light and conversational. The goal is **oral fluency with the pattern**, not written accuracy.

# Slide 8: Grammar — Key Time Words

---

## ⏱️ Grammar — Key Time Words for the Present Perfect
**EVER · NEVER · ALREADY · YET · JUST**

---

These five words are **strongly associated** with the Present Perfect. Learning where to place them is essential.

---

### 1. EVER

**Meaning:** *alguma vez* — used to ask about life experiences (at any time in your life)
**Position:** Between the subject and the past participle (after *have/has*)
**Used in:** Questions (and negative sentences for emphasis)

| # | Example |
|---|---------|
| 1 | *"Have you **ever** used a smartwatch?"* |
| 2 | *"Has she **ever** sent an email in English?"* |

> 🔑 Rule of thumb: **EVER = questions about experience**

---

### 2. NEVER

**Meaning:** *nunca* — used to say you have no experience of something
**Position:** Between the subject and the past participle (after *have/has*)
**Used in:** Affirmative sentences (grammatically positive, meaning negative)

| # | Example |
|---|---------|
| 1 | *"I have **never** used TikTok."* |
| 2 | *"He has **never** sent a voice message."* |

> 🔑 Rule of thumb: **NEVER = no experience at any time**
> ⚠️ *Never* already contains the negative meaning — don't add *not*:
> ❌ *"I haven't never used it."* → ✅ *"I have never used it."*

---

### 3. ALREADY

**Meaning:** *já* — something happened sooner than expected, or before a particular moment
**Position:** Between the auxiliary and the past participle (or at the end for emphasis)
**Used in:** Affirmative sentences

| # | Example |
|---|---------|
| 1 | *"I've **already** downloaded the app."* |
| 2 | *"She has **already** updated her profile."* |

> 🔑 Rule of thumb: **ALREADY = it happened (possibly surprising or ahead of schedule)**
> Can also go at the end: *"I've downloaded it **already**."*

---

### 4. YET

**Meaning:** *ainda / ainda não* — something expected hasn't happened, or asking if it happened
**Position:** At the **END** of the sentence
**Used in:** Negative sentences and questions

| # | Example |
|---|---------|
| 1 | *"I haven't received your message **yet**."* (negative) |
| 2 | *"Have you installed the app **yet**?"* (question) |

> 🔑 Rule of thumb: **YET = we expect something to happen, but it hasn't (or: has it happened?)**
> ⚠️ Always at the END of the sentence.
> ✅ *"I haven't received it **yet**."*

---

### 5. JUST

**Meaning:** *acabou de / acabei de* — something happened very recently
**Position:** Between the auxiliary and the past participle
**Used in:** Affirmative sentences

| # | Example |
|---|---------|
| 1 | *"I've **just** received your email!"* |
| 2 | *"He has **just** installed the update."* |

> 🔑 Rule of thumb: **JUST = a few seconds/minutes ago — right now**
> This is one of the most natural uses of Present Perfect in everyday English!

---

### 📊 Summary Table

| Word | Meaning (PT) | Position | Used in | Example |
|------|-------------|----------|---------|---------|
| **EVER** | alguma vez | after have/has | Questions | *"Have you **ever** tried this?"* |
| **NEVER** | nunca | after have/has | Affirmative | *"I've **never** used it."* |
| **ALREADY** | já | after have/has | Affirmative | *"I've **already** sent it."* |
| **YET** | ainda (não) | END of sentence | Negative / Question | *"I haven't done it **yet**."* |
| **JUST** | acabar de | after have/has | Affirmative | *"I've **just** connected!"* |

---

> 📌 **Teacher:** These five words are the most valuable "hooks" for helping students produce natural Present Perfect sentences. A practical memory tip to share:
>
> - *"EVER and NEVER — after 'have'. Questions and 'no experience'."*
> - *"ALREADY — middle of the sentence. It's done!"*
> - *"YET — always at the end. Not done, but expected."*
> - *"JUST — middle. Done right now, this second."*
>
> Quick spoken mini-drill: give a situation, student adds the correct word.
> - Teacher: *"You checked your email 2 minutes ago."* → Student: *"I've **just** checked my email."*
> - Teacher: *"You asked if I set up the app — and I did it an hour ago, before you asked."* → Student: *"I've **already** set it up."*

# Slide 9: Grammar — Present Perfect vs. Simple Past

---

## 🔄 Grammar — Present Perfect vs. Simple Past
**Understanding when to use each tense**

---

### The Key Difference

| | Present Perfect | Simple Past |
|-|----------------|-------------|
| **Time** | No specific time / unspecified | **Specific time** in the past |
| **Focus** | Connection to present / experience | Finished, completed action |
| **Common time words** | ever, never, already, yet, just, recently, before, so far | yesterday, last week, last year, in 2020, two days ago, at 3pm |
| **Question word** | **Have/Has** + subject...? | **Did** + subject...? |

---

### Side-by-Side Sentence Pairs

| Situation | Present Perfect | Simple Past |
|-----------|----------------|-------------|
| Experience (no time given) | *"I **have used** this app."* | *"I **used** this app **yesterday**."* |
| Something just happened | *"I've **just received** your email."* | *"I **received** your email **at 9am**."* |
| Asking about life experience | *"**Have** you **ever been** to the US?"* | *"**Did** you **go** to the US **last year**?"* |
| Result connected to now | *"She **has updated** her profile."* (it's different now) | *"She **updated** her profile **on Monday**."* |

---

### 🔑 Simple Rule for Now

> **No time word?** → Think about Present Perfect.
> **Specific time word (yesterday, last week, in 2020)?** → Use Simple Past.

---

### ⚠️ Common Student Errors

| ❌ Incorrect | ✅ Correct | Why |
|-------------|----------|-----|
| *"I have sent it yesterday."* | *"I **sent** it yesterday."* | *Yesterday* = specific time → Simple Past |
| *"Did you ever use Spotify?"* | *"**Have** you **ever** used Spotify?"* | *Ever* with life experience → Present Perfect |
| *"I have been to the US in 2019."* | *"I **went** to the US **in 2019**."* | *In 2019* = specific time → Simple Past |
| *"She has called me last night."* | *"She **called** me last night."* | *Last night* = specific time → Simple Past |

---

### 💡 Practical Tip

> 🇧🇷 **For Portuguese speakers:** In Brazilian Portuguese, you might say *"Eu usei esse app"* for both situations. In English, the tense changes depending on whether you mention a specific time. Pay attention to time expressions — they tell you which tense to use!

---

> 📌 **Teacher:** At the A1/A2 level, the goal is **recognition**, not perfect production. Students will naturally default to Simple Past, and that's okay. Your focus here is to:
> 1. Show the student that these are two different tools for different situations
> 2. Help them recognize the Present Perfect when they hear/read it
> 3. Teach the EVER/NEVER/ALREADY/YET/JUST words as reliable signals
>
> Say: *"When you see 'ever', 'never', 'already', 'yet', or 'just' — that's your signal to use Present Perfect. When you see 'yesterday' or 'last week' — use Simple Past. Simple as that."*

# Slide 10: Grammar — Modal CAN for Technology

---

## 💡 Grammar — Modal CAN for Technology Ability
**Talking about what you can and can't do with technology**

---

### CAN — Structure Table

| Form | Structure | Example |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **Positive** | Subject + **can** + base verb | *"I **can** upload photos from my phone."* |
| **Negative** | Subject + **can't** + base verb | *"I **can't** download anything. No Wi-Fi."* |
| **Question** | **Can** + subject + base verb + ? | *"**Can** you send me the file?"* |
| **Short Answer (+)** | Yes, + subject + **can**. | *"Can you connect? — Yes, I **can**."* |
| **Short Answer (–)** | No, + subject + **can't**. | *"Can she log in? — No, she **can't**."* |

---

### 📱 Useful Tech Expressions with CAN

1. *"You **can** use this app for free."*
2. *"I **can't** connect to the internet right now."*
3. *"**Can** you send me that link?"*
4. *"This phone **can't** take good photos."*
5. *"**Can** I use your charger?"*
6. *"You **can** log in with your Google account."*

---

### ⚙️ CAN + Technology Verb Combinations

| CAN + Verb | Example |
|-----------|---------|
| can + **download** | *"You **can download** the app for free."* |
| can + **connect** | *"**Can** your laptop **connect** to Bluetooth?"* |
| can + **send** | *"**Can** you **send** me the document?"* |
| can + **share** | *"You **can share** your location from this app."* |
| can + **stream** | *"**Can** you **stream** in 4K on this connection?"* |
| can + **install** | *"You **can install** this on any device."* |
| can + **log in** | *"**Can** you **log in** with Facebook?"* |

---

### ⚠️ Critical Error to Avoid

| ❌ Incorrect | ✅ Correct | Rule |
|-------------|----------|------|
| *"I can **to** send the file."* | *"I can **send** the file."* | Modal verbs NEVER take *to* — always use base verb |
| *"She cans upload it."* | *"She **can** upload it."* | *Can* is always the same — no -s for third person |
| *"Can you to help me?"* | *"Can you help me?"* | No *to* after *can* in questions |

> 🔑 **Remember:** After **can**, always use the **base form** of the verb — never add *to*, never add *-s*, never add *-ing*.

---

### 🔗 CAN vs. PRESENT PERFECT — Quick Comparison

- *"I **can** use Instagram."* → Ability (I know how to use it)
- *"I **have used** Instagram."* → Experience (I have done it before)

- **CAN** = *you are able to do it* (ability, permission, possibility)
- **Present Perfect** = *you did it at some point in your life* (experience/result)

---

> 📌 **Teacher:** Students at this level already know *can* from previous lessons. This slide reinforces CAN in a technology context. The key correction to drill is the **"can to"** error — very common with Portuguese speakers who transfer the *"poder + infinitivo"* structure directly.
>
> Practice naturally: *"Can your phone take videos? Can you install apps without Wi-Fi? Can you connect your laptop to the TV?"*

## 💬 Language in Context — Model Dialogues
**Seeing Present Perfect and CAN in real conversations**

---

### Dialogue 1: Sharing an App

> **Marcus:** Hey, have you ever used Duolingo?
>
> **Laura:** Yes, I have! I use it to practice Spanish. Have you tried it?
>
> **Marcus:** No, I haven't. Is it good?
>
> **Laura:** It's great! You can download it for free. It has a lot of exercises.
>
> **Marcus:** Really? I'll download it tonight. Can it connect to Facebook?
>
> **Laura:** Yes, it can. You can log in with your Facebook account.
>
> **Marcus:** Perfect. I've already installed it! Thanks for the tip.

---

### 🔍 Dialogue 1 — Structure Annotations

| Line | Structure | Grammar Point |
|------|-----------|---------------|
| *"Have you ever used Duolingo?"* | Have + you + ever + past participle | **Present Perfect Question** with *ever* |
| *"Yes, I have!"* | Short answer | **Present Perfect Short Answer** (positive) |
| *"Have you tried it?"* | Have + you + past participle | **Present Perfect Question** |
| *"No, I haven't."* | Short answer | **Present Perfect Short Answer** (negative) |
| *"You **can** download it for free."* | can + base verb | **Modal CAN** — possibility |
| *"**Can** it connect to Facebook?"* | Can + subject + base verb | **CAN Question** |
| *"Yes, it **can**."* | Short answer | **CAN Short Answer** |
| *"You **can** log in with Facebook."* | can + base verb | **Modal CAN** — ability |
| *"I've **already** installed it!"* | have + already + past participle | **Present Perfect** with *already* |

---

### Dialogue 2: Tech Problems

> **Ana:** Can you receive emails on your phone?
>
> **Bruno:** Yes, I can, but I haven't received anything today. My internet is not working.
>
> **Ana:** Have you tried to restart your phone?
>
> **Bruno:** Yes, I have. It didn't work. I can't connect to the Wi-Fi.
>
> **Ana:** What about using mobile data?
>
> **Bruno:** Oh! I haven't tried that. Good idea. Let me check... Yes! It's working now. I've just received your email.
>
> **Ana:** Great! Have you read it yet?
>
> **Bruno:** No, I haven't. I'll read it right now.

---

### 🔍 Dialogue 2 — Structure Annotations

| Line | Structure | Grammar Point |
|------|-----------|---------------|
| *"Can you receive emails on your phone?"* | Can + subject + base verb | **CAN Question** — ability |
| *"Yes, I can, but I **haven't received** anything today."* | haven't + past participle | **Present Perfect Negative** |
| *"**Have you tried** to restart your phone?"* | Have + subject + past participle | **Present Perfect Question** |
| *"Yes, I **have**."* | Short answer | **Present Perfect Short Answer** |
| *"It **didn't** work."* | didn't + base verb | **Simple Past** (specific past action) |
| *"I **can't** connect to the Wi-Fi."* | can't + base verb | **CAN Negative** |
| *"I **haven't tried** that."* | haven't + past participle | **Present Perfect Negative** |
| *"I've **just received** your email."* | have + just + past participle | **Present Perfect** with *just* |
| *"**Have you read** it **yet**?"* | Have + subject + past participle + yet | **Present Perfect Question** with *yet* |
| *"No, I **haven't**."* | Short answer | **Present Perfect Short Answer** (negative) |

### 📌 How to Use These Dialogues in Class

> 📌 **Teacher:** Do NOT assign the dialogues as role-play or student tasks. Instead:
>
> **Step 1 — First read-through:** Read both parts of Dialogue 1 aloud yourself (or alternate voices), while the student follows along. Keep a natural, conversational pace.
>
> **Step 2 — Comprehension check (verbal, teacher-led):** Ask simple questions:
> - *"Did Marcus know Duolingo before? No — so he said: 'No, I haven't.'"*
> - *"Did Laura already download it? Yes — she said: 'Yes, I have!'"*
>
> **Step 3 — Draw attention to grammar structures:** Point to the annotation table. Say: *"Look — every time we see 'have/has + past participle', that's Present Perfect. Count how many times it appears."*
>
> **Step 4 — Repeat with Dialogue 2:** Follow the same steps. Note how *Simple Past* ("It didn't work") appears alongside Present Perfect — this naturally reinforces the contrast from Slide 9.
>
> **Step 5 — Natural reading (optional):** If the student is engaged and confident, offer to read Dialogue 2 again with the student reading one character and the teacher the other — only if the student seems comfortable. Never pressure.

### 📐 Grammar Cheat Sheet

**Present Perfect Formula:**
```
Subject  +  have / has  +  past participle
```

| Form | Example |
|------|---------|
| Affirmative | *"I **have downloaded** the app."* |
| Negative | *"She **hasn't received** the message yet."* |
| Question | *"**Have** you **ever** used this?"* |
| Short answer (+) | *"Yes, I **have**."* |
| Short answer (–) | *"No, I **haven't**."* |

**Key Time Words:**

| Word | Meaning | Quick Rule |
|------|---------|------------|
| EVER | alguma vez | questions about experience |
| NEVER | nunca | no experience (affirmative sentence) |
| ALREADY | já | it happened (positive surprise) |
| YET | ainda não | not done yet — END of sentence |
| JUST | acabar de | happened right now — after have/has |

**Modal CAN:**
```
Subject  +  can / can't  +  base verb (NO "to")
```
*"I **can send** the file."* / *"She **can't connect** to Wi-Fi."* / *"**Can** you **install** this app?"*

### ⚠️ Common Errors to Watch For (6 Specific Errors + Corrections)

**Error 1: Adding "to" after modal CAN**
- ❌ *"I can to send the file."*
- ✅ *"I can send the file."*
- 💡 Tip: Remind the student: *"Can is special — no 'to' after it, ever."* Write it on paper if needed: **CAN + base verb (never TO)**.

**Error 2: Using Simple Past instead of Present Perfect with EVER/NEVER/ALREADY/YET/JUST**
- ❌ *"Did you ever try this app?"*
- ✅ *"Have you ever tried this app?"*
- 💡 Tip: Point out the trigger word *ever* and associate it directly with *have/has*: "EVER → HAVE/HAS."

**Error 3: Using Present Perfect with a specific time expression**
- ❌ *"I have seen this movie yesterday."*
- ✅ *"I saw this movie yesterday."*
- 💡 Tip: Teach the student to look for time words first: *yesterday, last week, in 2020, ago* → always Simple Past.

**Error 4: Contracting the positive short answer**
- ❌ *"Yes, I've."* / ❌ *"Yes, she's."*
- ✅ *"Yes, I have."* / ✅ *"Yes, she has."*
- 💡 Tip: Explain that English does NOT contract the last word in a short answer. *"Yes, I have"* — the "have" must be clear and full.

**Error 5: Using "have" instead of "has" for third person singular**
- ❌ *"She have connected."* / ❌ *"He haven't tried it."*
- ✅ *"She has connected."* / ✅ *"He hasn't tried it."*
- 💡 Tip: Quick reminder — *he/she/it → has/hasn't*. All others → *have/haven't*.

**Error 6: Using wrong preposition with technology verbs**
- ❌ *"Connect the internet."* / ❌ *"Search the restaurant."* / ❌ *"Log out your account."*
- ✅ *"Connect **to** the internet."* / ✅ *"Search **for** the restaurant."* / ✅ *"Log out **of** your account."*
- 💡 Tip: Create a small chant: *"Connect TO, search FOR, log out OF."*

### 🎓 Pedagogical Tips — Teaching Present Perfect to A1/A2 Learners

**1. Meaning before form**
Don't open with the formula. Open with the *concept*: *"We use this tense to talk about experiences — things you have done in your life. Not when. Just if."* Students learn the purpose first, then the form.

**2. Anchor to personal experience**
The Present Perfect becomes memorable when students use it about themselves. Ask: *"Have you ever eaten sushi? Have you ever used a VPN? Have you ever sent an email in English?"* These are real questions with real answers.

**3. Don't drill the Present Perfect vs. Simple Past contrast too hard**
At A1/A2, the most important thing is that students can form and recognize the Present Perfect. The contrast with Simple Past is important to introduce (Slide 9) but should not dominate the lesson. Students at this level will default to Simple Past — gently correct over time, not in one lesson.

**4. Key words (EVER, NEVER, etc.) are the shortcut**
For a beginner, remembering the formula is hard. But if you can teach them that *ever, never, already, yet, just* signal the Present Perfect, they have a practical tool to use immediately. Reinforce these words in every example.

**5. Contractions are natural — teach them from day one**
Learners often say *"I have used..."* when native speakers say *"I've used..."* Teach contractions as the standard form in speech, not as a shortcut. *"I've"* is not lazy — it's correct.

**6. The "just" form is the most immediately natural**
Among the five key words, *just* produces the most natural-sounding sentences for students right away: *"I've just received your message."* Use this as a confidence builder early in the lesson.
