## 📚 Grammar — Structure 1: Present Perfect (Introduction)

### What is the Present Perfect?

The **Present Perfect** connects the **past** with the **present**. We use it to talk about:
- Things that happened at an unspecified time before now
- Experiences in your life
- Things that happened recently and are still relevant

> *"I **have used** WhatsApp." → You used it sometime in your life, and it's still relevant now.*

---

### How to Form the Present Perfect

**Formula:** Subject + **have / has** + past participle

| Subject | Auxiliary | Verb (past participle) |
|---------|-----------|------------------------|
| I | **have** | used, sent, downloaded |
| You | **have** | used, sent, downloaded |
| He / She / It | **has** | used, sent, downloaded |
| We / They | **have** | used, sent, downloaded |

---

### ✅ Affirmative

| Structure | Example |
|-----------|---------|
| I **have** + past participle | "I **have used** this app before." |
| He/She **has** + past participle | "She **has received** your message." |

More examples:
- "I **have downloaded** a new app."
- "He **has uploaded** his photos."
- "We **have connected** to the Wi-Fi."
- "She **has sent** an email."

**Contractions (very common in spoken English):**
- I **have** → I**'ve** → "I've used this app."
- She **has** → She**'s** → "She's received your message."
- They **have** → They**'ve** → "They've connected to the internet."

---

### ❌ Negative

**Formula:** Subject + **have/has + not** + past participle

| Structure | Example |
|-----------|---------|
| I **haven't** + past participle | "I **haven't used** that app before." |
| He/She **hasn't** + past participle | "She **hasn't received** the email yet." |

More examples:
- "I **haven't downloaded** the update yet."
- "He **hasn't connected** to the internet."
- "We **haven't posted** anything today."

> **Key word: YET** → used with negatives to say something didn't happen *up to now*
> - "I **haven't read** your message **yet**."
> - "She **hasn't installed** the app **yet**."

---

### ❓ Questions

**Formula:** **Have/Has** + subject + past participle?

| Structure | Example |
|-----------|---------|
| **Have** you + past participle? | "**Have** you **used** this website before?" |
| **Has** he/she + past participle? | "**Has** she **received** the file?" |

More examples:
- "**Have** you **tried** this app?"
- "**Have** you **downloaded** the update?"
- "**Has** he **sent** the email?"

#### Short Answers
| Question | Yes | No |
|----------|-----|----|
| Have you used this app? | **Yes, I have.** | **No, I haven't.** |
| Has she received the message? | **Yes, she has.** | **No, she hasn't.** |

---

### 📌 Key word: EVER

**Ever** = "alguma vez" → used in questions to ask about life experiences

- "**Have** you **ever** used Instagram?"
- "**Have** you **ever** sent an email in English?"
- "**Has** he **ever** downloaded a virus?"

> **Ever** goes between the subject and the past participle in questions.

---

---

### ⚠️ Present Perfect vs. Simple Past

A very common question: *When do I use each?*

| Present Perfect | Simple Past |
|----------------|-------------|
| No specific time | Specific time |
| Life experience / recent event | Completed, finished action |
| Uses: ever, never, already, yet, just | Uses: yesterday, last week, in 2020, ago |

| ✅ Present Perfect | ✅ Simple Past |
|-------------------|--------------|
| "I **have sent** an email." | "I **sent** an email **yesterday**." |
| "She **has used** this app." | "She **used** this app **last night**." |
| "**Have** you **ever tried** TikTok?" | "**Did** you **try** TikTok **last year**?" |

> **Rule of thumb:** If you mention a specific time → use Simple Past.
> If time is unknown or not important → use Present Perfect.

## 📚 Grammar — Structure 2: Modal "CAN" for Ability with Technology

### What does "can" express?

**Can** expresses **ability** — what you are able to do.

When talking about technology, **can** is very useful:

- "I **can** send messages with this app."
- "You **can** download movies with this app."
- "This phone **can** connect to Bluetooth."

---

### How to Form "CAN"

**Positive:** Subject + **can** + base verb

| Subject | Can | Verb | Example |
|---------|-----|------|---------|
| I | can | upload | "I **can upload** photos from my phone." |
| You | can | connect | "You **can connect** to Wi-Fi here." |
| He/She | can | stream | "She **can stream** videos without Wi-Fi." |

---

**Negative:** Subject + **cannot / can't** + base verb

| Subject | Can't | Verb | Example |
|---------|-------|------|---------|
| I | can't | download | "I **can't download** anything. No Wi-Fi." |
| He | can't | connect | "He **can't connect** to this network." |

---

**Question:** **Can** + subject + base verb?

| Structure | Example | Short Answer |
|-----------|---------|--------------|
| **Can** + you + verb? | "**Can** you **send** me the file?" | "Yes, I can. / No, I can't." |
| **Can** + this app + verb? | "**Can** this app **translate** text?" | "Yes, it can. / No, it can't." |

---

### Useful "Can" Expressions with Technology

| Expression | Meaning |
|-----------|---------|
| "You **can** use this app for free." | O app é gratuito |
| "I **can't** connect to the internet." | Sem conexão |
| "**Can** you send me that link?" | Pedido educado |
| "This phone **can't** take good photos." | Limitação do dispositivo |
| "**Can** I use your charger?" | Pedido de permissão |

## 🗣️ Model Dialogue

### Dialogue 1: Sharing an App

**Marcus:** Hey, have you ever used Duolingo?

**Laura:** Yes, I have! I use it to practice Spanish. Have you tried it?

**Marcus:** No, I haven't. Is it good?

**Laura:** It's great! You can download it for free. It has a lot of exercises.

**Marcus:** Really? I'll download it tonight. Can it connect to Facebook?

**Laura:** Yes, it can. You can log in with your Facebook account.

**Marcus:** Perfect. I've already installed it! Thanks for the tip.

---

### Dialogue 2: Tech Problems

**Ana:** Can you receive emails on your phone?

**Bruno:** Yes, I can, but I haven't received anything today. My internet is not working.

**Ana:** Have you tried to restart your phone?

**Bruno:** Yes, I have. It didn't work. I can't connect to the Wi-Fi.

**Ana:** What about using mobile data?

**Bruno:** Oh! I haven't tried that. Good idea. Let me check... Yes! It's working now. I've just received your email.

**Ana:** Great! Have you read it yet?

**Bruno:** No, I haven't. I'll read it right now.

## 📖 Reading: Technology in Our Daily Lives

Technology has changed how we communicate. Today, most people **have** already **replaced** phone calls with text messages and emails. Social media **has** also **become** an essential part of life — people **have** started using apps like Instagram, TikTok, and Twitter to share news, photos, and videos.

Smartphones are powerful tools. With a good phone, you **can** do almost everything: you **can** send files, stream music, connect to friends anywhere in the world, and even learn a new language. Many people **have** never **sent** a letter in the mail — they **have** always **used** email or messaging apps.

However, technology **has** also **created** new problems. People spend too much time on their screens, and many people **have** lost the habit of talking face to face.

**Comprehension Questions:**
1. What have people replaced phone calls with?
2. What can you do with a good smartphone?
3. What problem has technology created?
4. Have you ever sent a letter by mail? (Personal answer)

## 🌍 Cultural Note: Digital Communication in the English-Speaking World

In countries like the United States, the United Kingdom, Canada, and Australia, digital communication is extremely common in professional and personal life.

**📧 Email etiquette:**
In professional settings, emails in English follow a specific structure:
- A greeting: *"Dear [Name],"* or *"Hi [Name],"*
- A polite opening: *"I hope this email finds you well."*
- The main message
- A closing: *"Best regards,"* / *"Kind regards,"* / *"Sincerely,"*

**💬 Messaging culture:**
- English speakers frequently use abbreviations in messages: *lol (laugh out loud), brb (be right back), btw (by the way), omg (oh my god), imo (in my opinion)*
- Voice messages (audio notes) are common but less professional than in Brazil — many people prefer written text

**📱 Social media:**
- English is the most widely used language on social media globally
- Hashtags (#) in English reach a global audience
- Commenting in English on international posts is a great way to practice the language
