## SLIDE 2: Warm-Up Activity 🎯 — Thesis Statement Challenge

### Individual Activity — 60 Seconds Per Topic

*[Teacher note: This is a solo warm-up. Display one topic at a time on the screen or read it aloud. Give each student exactly 60 seconds to write one argumentative sentence — a thesis statement — about the topic. After each round, ask one or two volunteers to read theirs aloud. Give brief, constructive feedback before moving to the next topic. Do not correct every sentence in detail; focus on whether the statement is debatable, specific, and contains one clear claim. This activity primes students to think critically before diving into academic writing structures.]*

---

### Topics on Screen — Choose 3–4 for the warm-up:

1. 📱 **Social media** — Is social media doing more harm than good to society?
2. 🤖 **AI in education** — Should artificial intelligence be integrated into classroom learning?
3. 🏠 **Remote work** — Does working from home reduce productivity?
4. 🌍 **Climate policy** — Should governments impose stricter regulations on carbon emissions?
5. 💰 **Universal basic income** — Would a universal basic income reduce poverty or discourage work?
6. 🔒 **Online privacy** — Should governments have access to citizens' private digital communications?

---

### What Makes a Strong Thesis Statement?

A strong thesis statement must be:
- ✅ **Debatable** — someone could reasonably disagree with it
- ✅ **Specific** — it makes a precise, focused claim
- ✅ **One clear argument** — it does not try to cover too many ideas at once
- ✅ **Positioned** — it takes a clear stance, not just a neutral observation

---

### STRONG vs. WEAK Thesis Examples

| | Example | Why? |
|---|---|---|
| ✅ **STRONG** | *"The widespread use of social media has significantly increased rates of anxiety and depression among teenagers by promoting unrealistic standards of appearance and success."* | Specific, debatable, takes a clear position, identifies a cause-and-effect relationship |
| ✅ **STRONG** | *"Governments should implement a universal basic income not to replace employment, but to provide citizens with the economic security needed to pursue meaningful and productive work."* | Nuanced, specific, directly addresses a common counterargument |
| ❌ **WEAK** | *"Social media is bad for people."* | Too vague — what kind of people? In what way? How bad? No specificity |
| ❌ **WEAK** | *"Universal basic income is an interesting topic that many people have different opinions about."* | Not a position at all — this is an observation, not an argument |

*[Teacher note: After reviewing the table, ask students: "What is the difference between stating a fact and making an argument?" Reinforce that academic writing requires them to defend a position with evidence — not just report information. The thesis is the backbone of any argumentative essay.]*

## SLIDE 3: Key Vocabulary — Part 1 📚

### Words 1–5: thesis · argument · counterargument · evidence · cite

---

### 1. THESIS
**🇧🇷 Portuguese:** tese
**Register:** [Academic] [Formal]
**Definition:** A central claim or main argument that a piece of writing sets out to prove or defend.

**Example sentences:**
- *"The author's thesis is that climate change is primarily driven by industrial activity rather than natural variation."*
- *"Before you begin writing your essay, you must formulate a clear thesis that guides the entire argument."*

**Usage note:**
- In academic writing, "thesis" refers to the central argument of a paper (e.g., *a strong thesis*, *a clear thesis*, *the paper's thesis*).
- It can also refer to a long research paper written for a university degree (e.g., *she defended her doctoral thesis*). Context makes the meaning clear.
- Common collocation: **thesis statement** — the one or two sentences in the introduction that express the thesis.

**Origin/Curiosity:** From Greek *thesis* (θέσις), meaning "a proposition" or "something set down." The word entered English through Latin in the 16th century and has been central to academic discourse ever since.

---

### 2. ARGUMENT
**🇧🇷 Portuguese:** argumento
**Register:** [Academic] [Formal] [Professional]
**Definition:** A reason or set of reasons given to support or oppose a claim; a line of reasoning intended to persuade.

**Example sentences:**
- *"The strongest argument in favour of renewable energy is its long-term cost efficiency compared to fossil fuels."*
- *"Her argument was well-structured, supported by three independent sources and a clear logical progression."*

**Usage note:**
- In everyday English, "argument" often means a disagreement or fight. In academic English, it means a **reasoned position** — be careful not to confuse the two registers.
- Collocations: *make an argument*, *present an argument*, *build an argument*, *a compelling/weak/flawed argument*.
- False friend alert for Portuguese speakers: *argumento* in Portuguese also primarily means a reasoned point, but students may default to the colloquial sense of "briga" (fight). Remind them to use it in its intellectual sense here.

**Origin/Curiosity:** From Latin *argumentum*, derived from *arguere* — to make clear, to prove. Notice the connection to the English word "argue."

---

### 3. COUNTERARGUMENT
**🇧🇷 Portuguese:** contra-argumento
**Register:** [Academic] [Formal]
**Definition:** A position or point that opposes or challenges the main argument; an objection raised against a claim.

**Example sentences:**
- *"A common counterargument against remote work policies is that they reduce team cohesion and spontaneous collaboration."*
- *"A well-written academic essay does not ignore counterarguments — it addresses and refutes them."*

**Usage note:**
- Acknowledging counterarguments actually **strengthens** your writing. It shows critical thinking and intellectual honesty.
- Useful academic phrases: *"One counterargument suggests that..."*, *"Critics argue that... however..."*, *"While it could be objected that..., the evidence shows..."*
- Collocations: *raise a counterargument*, *address a counterargument*, *refute a counterargument*.

**Origin/Curiosity:** The prefix *counter-* comes from Latin *contra*, meaning "against." You'll find it in many academic and professional words: *counterproductive*, *counterpart*, *counterintuitive*.

---

### 4. EVIDENCE
**🇧🇷 Portuguese:** evidência / prova
**Register:** [Academic] [Formal] [Professional]
**Definition:** Facts, data, examples, or other information used to support a claim or argument.

**Example sentences:**
- *"The researchers presented compelling evidence that sleep deprivation significantly impairs cognitive performance."*
- *"Without evidence, an argument is nothing more than an opinion — and opinions alone do not constitute academic writing."*

**Usage note:**
- "Evidence" is an **uncountable noun** in English — you cannot say *"an evidence"* or *"evidences"*. Instead: *"a piece of evidence"*, *"several pieces of evidence"*, *"the evidence suggests"*.
- Collocations: *empirical evidence*, *statistical evidence*, *anecdotal evidence*, *strong/weak evidence*, *cite evidence*, *present evidence*.
- Important distinction: *evidence* supports a claim; *proof* establishes certainty. In science and academia, we rarely claim "proof" — we speak of *evidence*.

---

### 5. CITE
**🇧🇷 Portuguese:** citar
**Register:** [Academic] [Formal]
**Definition:** To formally reference or quote a source of information in academic or professional writing, giving credit to the original author.

**Example sentences:**
- *"When you cite a source, you must include the author's name, publication year, and page number in the correct format."*
- *"The paper cites over 40 peer-reviewed studies to support its central hypothesis."*

**Usage note:**
- Noun form: **citation** — *"Include a citation at the end of every quoted or paraphrased sentence."*
- Do not confuse *cite* with *site* (a location) or *sight* (vision) — these are homophones.
- Common academic phrases: *as cited in*, *cited by*, *the study cited above*, *cite your sources*.
- Collocations: *cite a source*, *cite an author*, *cite evidence*, *cite statistics*.

*[Teacher note: Pause here and ask students to give an example sentence using one of the five words. This quick oral check ensures comprehension before moving to the next vocabulary set. You can prompt: "Can you use 'counterargument' in a sentence about climate change?" or "What is a piece of evidence you might use to argue for remote work?"]*

## SLIDE 4: Key Vocabulary — Part 2 📚

### Words 6–10: paraphrase · summarize · analyze · evaluate · synthesize

---

### 6. PARAPHRASE
**🇧🇷 Portuguese:** parafrasear / reformular
**Register:** [Academic] [Formal]
**Definition:** To restate someone else's ideas in your own words while maintaining the original meaning, without changing the core content.

**Example sentences:**
- *"Rather than copying the author's exact words, you should paraphrase the main point and then cite the source."*
- *"The student paraphrased the research findings effectively, demonstrating a clear understanding of the material."*

**Usage note:**
- Paraphrasing is NOT just replacing words with synonyms — it involves **restructuring sentences** and **reprocessing meaning** in your own voice.
- A paraphrase still requires a citation — it is not a way to avoid crediting the source.
- Noun form: **paraphrase** (also a noun) — *"Her paraphrase of the statistic was accurate and concise."*
- Collocations: *paraphrase a passage*, *paraphrase correctly*, *paraphrase and cite*.

**Origin/Curiosity:** From Greek *paraphrasis* — *para* (alongside) + *phrazein* (to tell). It literally means "to tell alongside" the original — not to copy it, but to restate it in parallel.

---

### 7. SUMMARIZE
**🇧🇷 Portuguese:** resumir / sintetizar
**Register:** [Academic] [Formal] [Professional]
**Definition:** To present the main points of a text, speech, or topic in a shorter, condensed form.

**Example sentences:**
- *"At the end of each section, the author summarizes the key findings before introducing a new argument."*
- *"Students were asked to summarize the article in no more than 150 words, identifying the main argument and three supporting points."*

**Usage note:**
- A summary is shorter than the original and captures only the **main ideas** — not details, examples, or minor points.
- Noun form: **summary** — *"Please write a summary of the chapter for homework."*
- Difference from *paraphrase*: A paraphrase is usually the same length as the original passage; a summary is significantly **shorter**.
- Collocations: *summarize the findings*, *summarize the argument*, *summarize briefly*, *write a summary*.

---

### 8. ANALYZE
**🇧🇷 Portuguese:** analisar
**Register:** [Academic] [Formal] [Professional]
**Definition:** To examine something in detail, breaking it into components in order to understand its structure, meaning, or implications.

**Example sentences:**
- *"The researcher analyzed the survey data to identify patterns in student learning behaviour across different demographics."*
- *"In your essay, do not simply describe the poem — analyze how the author's use of imagery contributes to the central theme."*

**Usage note:**
- American English spelling: **analyze** | British English spelling: **analyse** — both are correct; maintain consistency within a document.
- Noun form: **analysis** (plural: **analyses**) — *"The analysis revealed three key trends."*
- Adjective form: **analytical** — *"She takes a very analytical approach to problem-solving."*
- Collocations: *analyze data*, *analyze results*, *critical analysis*, *in-depth analysis*, *analyze the impact of*.

**Origin/Curiosity:** From Greek *analyein* — to unloose, to dissolve. The idea is of breaking something complex into its constituent parts to understand it — like dissolving a compound to study its elements.

---

### 9. EVALUATE
**🇧🇷 Portuguese:** avaliar / examinar criticamente
**Register:** [Academic] [Formal] [Professional]
**Definition:** To make a judgment about the quality, significance, or effectiveness of something based on criteria or evidence.

**Example sentences:**
- *"The committee was asked to evaluate each proposal based on feasibility, cost, and long-term impact."*
- *"In your conclusion, evaluate whether the evidence you presented successfully supports your thesis."*

**Usage note:**
- Evaluation involves **making a judgment** — it goes beyond describing or analyzing. You assess the *value*, *quality*, or *success* of something.
- Noun form: **evaluation** — *"The annual evaluation of teaching methods showed significant improvements."*
- Adjective form: **evaluative** — *"Use evaluative language in your conclusion to show critical thinking."*
- Collocations: *evaluate the effectiveness of*, *critically evaluate*, *evaluate evidence*, *evaluate performance*.
- Important: "evaluate" implies using a **standard or set of criteria** to make a judgment. This distinguishes it from simply giving an opinion.

---

### 10. SYNTHESIZE
**🇧🇷 Portuguese:** sintetizar / integrar
**Register:** [Academic] [Formal]
**Definition:** To combine information, ideas, or arguments from multiple sources into a coherent, unified whole that creates new understanding.

**Example sentences:**
- *"A strong literature review does not simply list sources — it synthesizes them to reveal patterns, contradictions, and gaps in the research."*
- *"The final chapter synthesizes the findings from all three experiments, presenting a unified theory of learning motivation."*

**Usage note:**
- Synthesis is the **highest-order academic skill** in this vocabulary set. It goes beyond summarizing (shorter version) or analyzing (breaking apart) — it involves **building something new** from multiple parts.
- Noun form: **synthesis** — *"The essay presents a clear synthesis of conflicting viewpoints."*
- Collocations: *synthesize information*, *synthesize sources*, *synthesize findings*, *a synthesis of research*.
- In Bloom's Taxonomy of learning, **synthesis/creation** is at the top — it requires understanding, analysis, and evaluation before it can be done effectively.

**Origin/Curiosity:** From Greek *synthesis* — *syn* (together) + *tithenai* (to place). To synthesize is literally to *place things together* — to bring separate elements into a new, unified structure.

*[Teacher note: After presenting Slide 4, consider doing a quick vocabulary sorting exercise verbally. Ask: "Which of these words describes taking something apart to understand it? Which describes putting multiple ideas together? Which is about reducing length?" This distinction — analyze (break apart) vs. synthesize (combine) vs. summarize (shorten) — is a crucial conceptual milestone for B2 students.]*

## SLIDE 5: Grammar Focus 1 — Advanced Nominalization 🔤

### What Is Nominalization?

**Nominalization** (nominalização) is the process of converting a **verb** or **adjective** into a **noun** in order to create a more formal, academic, and impersonal tone in writing.

Instead of writing: *"We need to achieve this goal."*
You write: *"The **achievement** of this goal is necessary."*

The meaning is the same — but the second version sounds more formal, more abstract, and more appropriate for academic or professional writing.

*[Teacher note: Stress that nominalization is not about making sentences harder to understand — it is about shifting the *register* (tom/registro) of the writing. Native academic writers use nominalization naturally. Your students should learn to recognize it when reading and use it deliberately when writing. However, warn against overusing it — overly nominalized writing can become dense and difficult to follow.]*

---

### Common Nominalization Suffixes

| Suffix | Example |
|--------|---------|
| **-tion / -sion** | discuss → discussion, achieve → achievement... wait, see below |
| **-ment** | achieve → achievement, develop → development |
| **-ance / -ence** | perform → performance, exist → existence |
| **-ity** | complex → complexity, possible → possibility |
| **-al** | approve → approval, propose → proposal |
| **-sis** | analyze → analysis, synthesize → synthesis |
| **-tion** | evaluate → evaluation, apply → application |
| **-sion** | conclude → conclusion, discuss → discussion |

---

### Verb → Nominalization Table

| Verb / Adjective | Nominalization | Part of Speech Shift |
|-----------------|----------------|----------------------|
| achieve | achievement | verb → noun |
| discuss | discussion | verb → noun |
| analyze | analysis | verb → noun |
| argue | argument | verb → noun |
| evaluate | evaluation | verb → noun |
| synthesize | synthesis | verb → noun |
| conclude | conclusion | verb → noun |
| apply | application | verb → noun |
| require | requirement | verb → noun |
| develop | development | verb → noun |
| significant | significance | adjective → noun |
| possible | possibility | adjective → noun |

---

### Before & After: Nominalization in Action

**1.**
- ❌ *Before (informal):* "We failed because we didn't communicate properly."
- ✅ *After (academic):* "The **failure** was attributed to a **lack of communication**."

**2.**
- ❌ *Before (informal):* "Scientists analyzed the data and found interesting patterns."
- ✅ *After (academic):* "The **analysis** of the data revealed several noteworthy patterns."

**3.**
- ❌ *Before (informal):* "When we require more resources, the project slows down."
- ✅ *After (academic):* "Increased **resource requirements** lead to a **reduction** in project efficiency."

**4.**
- ❌ *Before (informal):* "Countries should develop their economies in a sustainable way."
- ✅ *After (academic):* "Sustainable **economic development** remains a central priority for emerging nations."

---

*[Teacher note: Notice that in the academic versions, the sentences often become slightly longer — that is normal and acceptable. The key change is that the **action word (verb)** becomes a **thing (noun)**, and the sentence restructures around that noun. Ask students to identify the nominalization in each "after" sentence before revealing the answer.]*

*[Teacher note: Common student error — nominalizing everything. Over-nominalization produces dense, bureaucratic text that is difficult to read. A good rule of thumb is: if a sentence becomes hard to follow due to too many abstract nouns, rewrite with a more active structure. Academic writing should be precise AND readable.]*

## SLIDE 6: Grammar Focus 2 — Academic Hedging 🎓

### What Is Hedging?

**Hedging** (linguagem cautelosa / mitigação) refers to the use of linguistic devices to express **caution**, **uncertainty**, or **tentativeness** when making claims in academic or professional writing.

In academic writing, very few things are absolute. Science advances through evidence and probability — not certainty. Hedging allows writers to:
- Present findings **without overclaiming**
- Acknowledge the **limits of their evidence**
- Sound more **credible and rigorous** (not weaker!)
- Avoid being proven wrong on technical grounds

> *"Hedging is not a sign of weakness — it is a mark of intellectual honesty and academic maturity."*

*[Teacher note: Many students at B2 level feel that hedging makes their writing sound uncertain or unconvincing. Address this directly: in academic culture, a writer who overclaims (e.g., "This proves that...") is seen as less credible than one who hedges appropriately (e.g., "This suggests that..."). The goal is precision, not confidence.]*

---

### Category 1: Probability & Possibility

These expressions suggest that something is likely but not certain:

| Expression | Example |
|-----------|---------|
| *It appears that...* | *It appears that increased screen time is correlated with reduced attention spans.* |
| *It seems likely that...* | *It seems likely that remote work will remain a permanent feature of modern employment.* |
| *This may suggest...* | *This may suggest a causal relationship between sleep deprivation and academic performance.* |
| *It is possible that...* | *It is possible that the results were affected by external variables not controlled in the study.* |
| *There is a possibility that...* | *There is a possibility that the intervention had an unintended effect on the control group.* |

---

### Category 2: Attribution to Sources

These expressions attribute a claim to a source rather than stating it as personal fact:

| Expression | Example |
|-----------|---------|
| *According to...* | *According to Smith (2021), the policy had a measurable effect on urban poverty rates.* |
| *Research indicates that...* | *Research indicates that bilingual students tend to outperform monolingual peers on executive function tasks.* |
| *The data suggests...* | *The data suggests a strong correlation between early childhood nutrition and cognitive development.* |
| *Studies have shown that...* | *Studies have shown that physical activity improves memory consolidation in adolescents.* |
| *It has been argued that...* | *It has been argued that digital technology has fundamentally changed the nature of literacy.* |

---

### Category 3: Frequency & Generalization

These limit the scope of a claim, avoiding absolute generalizations:

| Expression | Example |
|-----------|---------|
| *In most cases...* | *In most cases, early intervention leads to significantly better outcomes.* |
| *This is often seen when...* | *This effect is often seen when variables are not properly controlled.* |
| *Typically...* | *Typically, economic growth is accompanied by increased energy consumption.* |
| *For the most part...* | *For the most part, the results align with existing literature on the subject.* |

---

### Category 4: Approximation

These soften numerical or factual claims:

| Expression | Example |
|-----------|---------|
| *approximately* | *Approximately 60% of respondents indicated a preference for flexible working hours.* |
| *roughly* | *Roughly half of the participants reported experiencing symptoms of anxiety.* |
| *around* | *The intervention lasted around six weeks across three different school sites.* |
| *in the region of* | *Costs were estimated to be in the region of $2 million annually.* |

---

### Over-Confident vs. Hedged: Side-by-Side Comparison

| ❌ Over-Confident | ✅ Hedged Version |
|------------------|-----------------|
| *"Social media causes depression."* | *"Research suggests that heavy social media use may be associated with increased rates of depression among adolescents."* |
| *"This proves that online learning is less effective."* | *"These findings indicate that online learning may be less effective than in-person instruction in certain contexts."* |
| *"Remote work always reduces productivity."* | *"In many reported cases, remote work appears to be associated with some reduction in collaborative productivity."* |

---

### When NOT to Hedge

*[Teacher note: This is a critical teaching point. Hedging is NOT appropriate in all situations. Teach students to distinguish:]*

- ❌ **Avoid hedging with established facts:**
  - Wrong: *"It appears that the Earth orbits the Sun."*
  - Right: *"The Earth orbits the Sun."*

- ❌ **Avoid hedging in strong conclusions supported by robust evidence:**
  - Wrong: *"These 12 independent studies might possibly suggest that smoking could perhaps be harmful."*
  - Right: *"The cumulative evidence strongly indicates that smoking is harmful to human health."*

- ✅ **Use hedging when presenting your own interpretations, predictions, or findings from limited data.**

## SLIDE 7: Grammar Focus 3 — Academic Paragraph Structure 📝

### The 4-Part Academic Paragraph

Every strong academic paragraph follows a clear, predictable structure. This consistency helps readers follow your argument and evaluate your reasoning. The four parts are:

---

### 1. 🟦 Topic Sentence (Frase Tópico)
**Purpose:** States the **main idea** of the paragraph — one clear, specific claim that everything else in the paragraph will support.

- It is usually the **first sentence** of the paragraph.
- It should be **arguable**, not a statement of obvious fact.
- It must connect to the **thesis** of the essay.

> *Example: "The integration of technology in classrooms has significantly transformed both the delivery and the accessibility of education."*

---

### 2. 🟨 Evidence (Evidência)
**Purpose:** Provides **data, statistics, quotations, examples, or citations** that support the topic sentence.

- Evidence is not your opinion — it comes from **external sources**.
- It should be **cited** properly.
- Use phrases like: *"According to...", "A 2022 study found that...", "For example...", "Data from... shows that..."*

> *Example: "According to a 2023 UNESCO report, 74% of secondary school students in high-income countries regularly use digital devices as their primary learning tool (UNESCO, 2023)."*

---

### 3. 🟧 Analysis (Análise)
**Purpose:** **Explains how** the evidence supports the topic sentence. This is **your voice** — your interpretation and reasoning.

- This is often the **most neglected** part by students.
- Do not assume the connection is obvious — explain it explicitly.
- Use phrases like: *"This demonstrates that...", "This suggests...", "The significance of this is...", "In other words..."*

> *Example: "This demonstrates that digital tools have become central to modern pedagogy, suggesting that schools unable to provide adequate technological access risk creating educational inequalities between students of different socioeconomic backgrounds."*

---

### 4. 🟩 Concluding Sentence (Frase de Conclusão)
**Purpose:** **Wraps up** the paragraph's argument and, when appropriate, **links** to the next paragraph or to the broader thesis.

- It should **restate the main idea** in different words — do not simply copy the topic sentence.
- It may **signal a transition** to the next point.
- Avoid introducing **new information** here.

> *Example: "It is clear, therefore, that the effective use of technology in education is no longer a supplementary feature but a foundational requirement for equitable academic achievement."*

---

### Complete Sample Paragraph — Annotated

> **[TS — Topic Sentence]** *The integration of technology in classrooms has significantly transformed both the delivery and the accessibility of education.*
>
> **[E — Evidence]** *According to a 2023 UNESCO report, 74% of secondary school students in high-income countries regularly use digital devices as their primary learning tool (UNESCO, 2023).*
>
> **[E — Evidence]** *Furthermore, a longitudinal study conducted across five countries found that students who had consistent access to educational technology scored, on average, 23% higher on standardized assessments than those without such access (Park & Nguyen, 2022).*
>
> **[A — Analysis]** *These findings demonstrate that digital tools have become central to contemporary pedagogy, and more critically, that unequal access to these tools may reinforce existing socioeconomic disparities in educational outcomes.*
>
> **[A — Analysis]** *The significance of this lies not merely in the technology itself, but in the capacity of educational institutions to integrate it effectively and equitably across diverse student populations.*
>
> **[C — Concluding Sentence]** *It is clear, therefore, that the effective use of technology in education is no longer a supplementary feature but a foundational requirement for equitable academic achievement — a point that will be explored further in the context of policy and resource allocation.*

---

### Common Student Mistakes

*[Teacher note: These are the most frequent errors at B2 level when writing academic paragraphs:]*

1. **Starting with evidence before the topic sentence** — Students often begin with a quote or statistic without stating the point first. Remind them: the reader needs to know the claim *before* seeing the support.

2. **Missing the analysis** — Students present evidence and then move directly to the next piece of evidence. The analysis is where *they* speak — it is the most important part for demonstrating critical thinking.

3. **Copying the topic sentence as the concluding sentence** — The conclusion should restate the idea in **new words** and add a sense of closure or transition.

4. **Overly long paragraphs** — An academic paragraph typically contains between 150–250 words. If a paragraph exceeds this, it likely contains more than one main idea and should be split.

5. **Weak topic sentences** — Topic sentences like *"There are many advantages to technology in education"* are too vague. A strong topic sentence makes a specific, arguable claim.

## SLIDE 8: Grammar Focus 4 — Passive Voice in Formal Writing ✍️

### Structure of the Passive Voice

**Formula:**
> **Subject + conjugated form of *to be* + Past Participle (+ *by* + agent — optional)**

| Active Voice | Passive Voice |
|-------------|--------------|
| *Researchers conducted the study.* | *The study was conducted by researchers.* |
| *The committee will review the applications.* | *The applications will be reviewed by the committee.* |
| *Scientists have analyzed the samples.* | *The samples have been analyzed.* |
| *They publish the results annually.* | *The results are published annually.* |

---

### When to Prefer Passive Voice in Academic Writing

#### 1. When the agent is unknown or unimportant
> *"The data was collected over a period of six months."*
(It does not matter *who* collected the data — the process is what matters.)

#### 2. To maintain objectivity and impersonality
> *"The hypothesis was tested using a double-blind procedure."*
(Using "we tested" would sound more personal — less appropriate for formal academic prose.)

#### 3. To focus on the process, result, or object — not the person
> *"Three independent variables were identified in the preliminary analysis."*
(The emphasis is on the variables — not on the researcher who identified them.)

#### 4. To avoid repeating the subject (stylistic flow)
> *"The participants completed the survey online. They were then asked to attend a follow-up interview."*
(The passive avoids repeating "participants" and creates smooth flow.)

---

### Passive Voice in Different Tenses

| Tense | Structure | Example |
|-------|-----------|---------|
| **Simple Present** | is/are + past participle | *The results are reviewed quarterly.* |
| **Simple Past** | was/were + past participle | *The experiment was conducted in 2021.* |
| **Future (will)** | will be + past participle | *The findings will be published next year.* |
| **Present Perfect** | has/have been + past participle | *The methodology has been revised twice.* |
| **Past Perfect** | had been + past participle | *The samples had been analyzed before the report was written.* |
| **Present Continuous** | is/are being + past participle | *The data is currently being processed.* |
| **Modal Verbs** | modal + be + past participle | *The results should be interpreted with caution.* |

---

### Active → Passive Transformations in Academic Sentences

**1.**
- 🔵 *Active:* "The research team identified three major risk factors."
- 🟠 *Passive:* "Three major risk factors were identified."

**2.**
- 🔵 *Active:* "Economists have argued that inflation is largely driven by supply chain disruptions."
- 🟠 *Passive:* "It has been argued that inflation is largely driven by supply chain disruptions."

**3.**
- 🔵 *Active:* "The university will implement the new curriculum in September."
- 🟠 *Passive:* "The new curriculum will be implemented in September."

**4.**
- 🔵 *Active:* "Multiple studies have demonstrated a strong correlation between poverty and poor health outcomes."
- 🟠 *Passive:* "A strong correlation between poverty and poor health outcomes has been demonstrated across multiple studies."

---

### Common Academic Passive Phrases

These are fixed or semi-fixed expressions widely used in academic English:

| Phrase | Use |
|--------|-----|
| *It has been argued that...* | Introducing a position in the literature |
| *It has been suggested that...* | Hedging + citing |
| *The results were analyzed using...* | Methodology description |
| *The study was conducted in...* | Research context |
| *The data was collected from...* | Methodology description |
| *It should be noted that...* | Signposting a caveat |
| *It is widely accepted that...* | Referring to consensus |
| *The hypothesis was supported by...* | Reporting results |
| *Further research is needed to...* | Acknowledging limitations |
| *This has been confirmed by...* | Citing supporting evidence |

*[Teacher note: A very common mistake at B2 level is passive voice overuse, which makes writing dense and hard to follow. A useful classroom guideline: if a paragraph has more than 3 consecutive passive voice sentences, it is worth reviewing for variety. Academic writing benefits from a **mix** of active and passive — active for strong, direct claims; passive for methodology, results, and impersonal statements. Ask students: "Which sentences in this slide would you rewrite in active voice? Why?"]*

### ⭐ 5 Golden Rules of Academic Writing

1. **Always support your claims with evidence.** An unsupported argument is just an opinion. Cite your sources, use data, and give examples from credible material.

2. **Be precise, not vague.** Academic writing values specificity. Instead of "many people," write "74% of respondents" or "a majority of the participants surveyed." Replace vague words with exact ones wherever possible.

3. **Hedge when you are not 100% certain.** Use cautious language (*suggests, appears, may indicate*) to qualify your claims. This is a mark of academic maturity, not weakness.

4. **Structure every paragraph around one clear idea.** Every paragraph must have a topic sentence, evidence, analysis, and a concluding sentence. If you are making two different points, write two separate paragraphs.

5. **Revise for register and tone.** Academic writing is formal and impersonal. Avoid contractions (*don't → do not*), colloquial expressions (*a lot of → a significant number of*), and first-person emotional language (*I feel that → It could be argued that*).
