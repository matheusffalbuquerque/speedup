## 1. **thesis**
- **IPA:** /ˈθiː.sɪs/
- **Part of speech:** Noun (plural: theses /ˈθiː.siːz/)
- **Tradução (PT-BR):** tese; dissertação
- **Register:** [Academic] [Formal]
- **Definition:** A central claim or main argument that a piece of writing sets out to prove or support. In academic contexts, it can also refer to a long research paper submitted for a university degree.
- **Common Collocations:**
  - develop a thesis
  - support / defend a thesis
  - thesis statement
- **Example Sentences:**
  1. The author's central **thesis** is that climate change is primarily driven by human industrial activity.
  2. Her doctoral **thesis** examined the relationship between social media use and adolescent mental health.
  3. A strong **thesis statement** should be specific, arguable, and supported by evidence throughout the essay.
- **Usage Note:** Do not confuse *thesis* (the central claim or academic paper) with *topic* (the subject being discussed). A *topic* is "climate change"; a *thesis* is "climate change is caused by human activity." The plural *theses* is often mispronounced — say /ˈθiː.siːz/, not "thesises."

## 2. **argument**
- **IPA:** /ˈɑːr.ɡjʊ.mənt/
- **Part of speech:** Noun
- **Tradução (PT-BR):** argumento; linha de raciocínio
- **Register:** [Academic] [Formal] [Professional]
- **Definition:** A set of reasons or evidence presented to support or justify a particular position or claim. In academic writing, an argument is logical and structured, not emotional.
- **Common Collocations:**
  - make / present an argument
  - a compelling / strong / weak argument
  - line of argument
- **Example Sentences:**
  1. The researcher presented a compelling **argument** for the reform of current immigration policies.
  2. His **argument** rests on three key pillars: empirical data, historical precedent, and logical inference.
  3. To write a persuasive essay, students must construct a clear and coherent **argument** from the outset.
- **Usage Note:** In everyday English, *argument* often means a heated disagreement between people. In academic English, it means a reasoned position. Be careful not to use *argument* to mean a fight in a formal text. The verb form is *argue*, and the adjective is *argumentative*.

## 3. **counterargument**
- **IPA:** /ˈkaʊn.tər.ɑːr.ɡjʊ.mənt/
- **Part of speech:** Noun
- **Tradução (PT-BR):** contra-argumento
- **Register:** [Academic] [Formal]
- **Definition:** A reason, claim, or line of reasoning that challenges or opposes the main argument being made. Acknowledging counterarguments strengthens academic writing by showing awareness of multiple perspectives.
- **Common Collocations:**
  - address a counterargument
  - anticipate / refute a counterargument
  - present the counterargument
- **Example Sentences:**
  1. The essay would be stronger if the writer addressed the main **counterargument** before presenting the conclusion.
  2. One **counterargument** to this position is that economic growth does not always lead to improved quality of life.
  3. Effective academic writers anticipate **counterarguments** and explain why their evidence is more convincing.
- **Usage Note:** A *counterargument* is not the same as a *rebuttal*. The *counterargument* is the opposing claim; the *rebuttal* is your response to it. In Portuguese, students sometimes say "contra-ponto" — the more precise academic term is *contra-argumento*.

## 4. **evidence**
- **IPA:** /ˈev.ɪ.dəns/
- **Part of speech:** Noun (uncountable)
- **Tradução (PT-BR):** evidência; prova
- **Register:** [Academic] [Formal] [Professional]
- **Definition:** Facts, data, examples, or expert opinions used to support a claim or argument. Evidence is the foundation of all academic and professional writing.
- **Common Collocations:**
  - provide / present evidence
  - empirical / statistical / anecdotal evidence
  - evidence suggests / indicates
- **Example Sentences:**
  1. The study provides compelling **evidence** that regular exercise reduces the risk of cardiovascular disease.
  2. Without supporting **evidence**, an argument is merely an opinion.
  3. The **evidence** presented in the final report was gathered through a combination of surveys and interviews.
- **Usage Note:** *Evidence* is uncountable in standard academic English — do NOT say "evidences" or "an evidence." The correct forms are "a piece of evidence," "some evidence," or "the evidence." *Proof* is stronger than *evidence*: proof means something is definitely true; evidence supports a claim.

## 5. **cite**
- **IPA:** /saɪt/
- **Part of speech:** Verb (noun form: citation /saɪˈteɪ.ʃən/)
- **Tradução (PT-BR):** citar; referenciar
- **Register:** [Academic] [Formal] [Professional]
- **Definition:** To quote or refer to a source — a book, article, study, or expert — as evidence for a claim. Citing sources gives credit to original authors and allows readers to verify information.
- **Common Collocations:**
  - cite a source / study / author
  - cite evidence / statistics
  - as cited in...
- **Example Sentences:**
  1. The report **cites** data from the World Health Organization to support its public health recommendations.
  2. Failure to **cite** your sources constitutes academic plagiarism.
  3. When writing a research paper, students must **cite** all secondary sources using the appropriate referencing style.
- **Usage Note:** Be careful not to confuse *cite* (to reference a source), *site* (a location), and *sight* (vision) — they are homophones in English. In Brazilian Portuguese, *citar* is a direct cognate, but the English word is used specifically in the context of academic referencing. The related noun is *citation*.

## 6. **paraphrase**
- **IPA:** /ˈpær.ə.freɪz/
- **Part of speech:** Verb / Noun
- **Tradução (PT-BR):** parafrasear; reformular com suas próprias palavras
- **Register:** [Academic] [Formal]
- **Definition:** To restate someone else's ideas or information using different words and sentence structures, while preserving the original meaning. Paraphrasing is a key academic skill that shows deep understanding of source material.
- **Common Collocations:**
  - paraphrase a passage / source
  - paraphrase accurately
  - a paraphrase of...
- **Example Sentences:**
  1. Rather than quoting the passage directly, the student chose to **paraphrase** the key findings in her own words.
  2. A good **paraphrase** does not simply replace words with synonyms; it restructures the sentence completely.
  3. When you **paraphrase**, you must still **cite** the original author.
- **Usage Note:** Paraphrasing is NOT the same as summarizing. A *paraphrase* covers all the details of the original passage in roughly the same length. A *summary* is shorter and covers only the main points. Also, changing a few words is NOT paraphrasing — that is patchwriting, which is considered a form of plagiarism.

## 7. **summarize**
- **IPA:** /ˈsʌm.ər.aɪz/
- **Part of speech:** Verb (noun form: summary /ˈsʌm.ər.i/)
- **Tradução (PT-BR):** resumir; sintetizar os pontos principais
- **Register:** [Academic] [Formal] [Professional]
- **Definition:** To express the main points of a text, speech, or argument in a shorter, condensed form. A summary omits details and examples, focusing only on the core ideas.
- **Common Collocations:**
  - summarize the main points / findings
  - briefly summarize
  - in summary...
- **Example Sentences:**
  1. At the end of the conference, the chairperson was asked to **summarize** the key conclusions reached by the panel.
  2. The executive report **summarizes** the findings of three years of market research.
  3. Students were required to **summarize** each chapter in no more than 100 words.
- **Usage Note:** *Summarize* (American English) and *summarise* (British English) are both correct. In B2 academic writing, avoid phrases like "In summary, I talked about…" — instead, write "In summary, this essay has argued that…" The noun *summary* is different from *conclusion*: a summary recaps what was said; a conclusion draws a final judgment.

## 8. **analyze** *(also: analyse — British English)*
- **IPA:** /ˈæn.ə.laɪz/
- **Part of speech:** Verb (noun form: analysis /əˈnæl.ɪ.sɪs/; adjective: analytical /ˌæn.əˈlɪt.ɪ.kəl/)
- **Tradução (PT-BR):** analisar
- **Register:** [Academic] [Formal] [Professional]
- **Definition:** To examine something in detail, breaking it down into its component parts to understand its nature, function, or meaning. Analysis goes beyond description — it explains *how* and *why*.
- **Common Collocations:**
  - analyze data / results / a text
  - critically analyze
  - analyze in depth / in detail
- **Example Sentences:**
  1. The researchers **analyzed** over 500 survey responses to identify patterns in consumer behavior.
  2. This essay will **analyze** the causes and consequences of the 2008 global financial crisis.
  3. Students are expected to **analyze** primary sources critically, not simply describe their content.
- **Usage Note:** Many students describe when they should *analyze*. Description says *what* happened; analysis says *why* it happened and *what it means*. The noun *analysis* has an irregular plural: *analyses* /əˈnæl.ɪ.siːz/. Do not say "analysises."

## 9. **evaluate**
- **IPA:** /ɪˈvæl.ju.eɪt/
- **Part of speech:** Verb (noun form: evaluation /ɪˌvæl.juˈeɪ.ʃən/)
- **Tradução (PT-BR):** avaliar; fazer uma avaliação crítica
- **Register:** [Academic] [Formal] [Professional]
- **Definition:** To make a careful judgment about the value, quality, effectiveness, or importance of something, based on specific criteria. Evaluation involves weighing strengths and weaknesses.
- **Common Collocations:**
  - evaluate the effectiveness / impact / evidence
  - critically evaluate
  - evaluate a claim / approach / study
- **Example Sentences:**
  1. The committee was asked to **evaluate** the effectiveness of the new public health campaign.
  2. Students must **evaluate** the reliability of their sources before including them in their essays.
  3. This paper **evaluates** three competing theories of language acquisition in young children.
- **Usage Note:** *Evaluate* is stronger and more systematic than *assess*. *To assess* can be informal; *to evaluate* implies using specific criteria to make a judgment. In essay questions, the instruction "evaluate" means you should discuss both strengths and limitations before reaching a conclusion.

## 10. **synthesize**
- **IPA:** /ˈsɪn.θɪ.saɪz/
- **Part of speech:** Verb (noun form: synthesis /ˈsɪn.θɪ.sɪs/)
- **Tradução (PT-BR):** sintetizar; integrar diferentes fontes ou ideias
- **Register:** [Academic] [Formal]
- **Definition:** To combine information, ideas, or arguments from multiple sources into a unified, coherent whole. Synthesis is the highest-level academic skill — it shows you can integrate multiple perspectives into an original insight.
- **Common Collocations:**
  - synthesize information / sources / research
  - synthesize findings
  - a synthesis of...
- **Example Sentences:**
  1. A literature review requires students to **synthesize** findings from multiple studies into a coherent narrative.
  2. The final chapter **synthesizes** the theoretical framework with the empirical data collected during the fieldwork.
  3. Rather than simply listing sources, effective academic writers **synthesize** them to build a persuasive argument.
- **Usage Note:** Synthesis is NOT a summary. A *summary* reduces one source; a *synthesis* integrates multiple sources to create something new. The noun *synthesis* has an irregular plural: *syntheses* /ˈsɪn.θɪ.siːz/. At B2 level and above, synthesis is considered an essential academic writing skill.

## 2.1 Nominalization

### What Is Nominalization?

**Nominalization** (nominalização) is the process of transforming verbs or adjectives into nouns. It is one of the most important features of academic and professional English, making writing more formal, dense, and precise.

> **Example:**
> - Informal: *The researchers discovered that the drug was effective.*
> - Academic (nominalized): *The researchers' **discovery** of the drug's **effectiveness** was significant.*

### Why Use Nominalization?
- ✅ Makes writing more formal and objective
- ✅ Allows more information in fewer sentences
- ✅ Creates distance between the writer and the claim (more academic tone)
- ✅ Reduces reliance on personal pronouns (I, we, they)

### Complete Nominalization Table (12+ transformations)

| Verb / Adjective | → Noun Form | Suffix Used |
|---|---|---|
| achieve | **achievement** | -ment |
| discuss | **discussion** | -sion |
| analyze | **analysis** | -sis |
| argue | **argument** | -ment |
| evaluate | **evaluation** | -tion |
| synthesize | **synthesis** | -sis |
| conclude | **conclusion** | -sion |
| apply | **application** | -tion |
| require | **requirement** | -ment |
| develop | **development** | -ment |
| investigate | **investigation** | -tion |
| propose | **proposal** | -al |
| significant | **significance** | -ance |
| evident | **evidence** | -ence |
| possible | **possibility** | -ity |

### Sentence Transformation Pairs

**Pair 1:**
- ❌ Before: *We investigated how students learn vocabulary.*
- ✅ After: ***The investigation** of student vocabulary acquisition revealed several key insights.*

**Pair 2:**
- ❌ Before: *She argued that the policy was flawed.*
- ✅ After: *Her **argument** concerning the policy's limitations was well-supported.*

**Pair 3:**
- ❌ Before: *The team developed a new approach to data collection.*
- ✅ After: ***The development** of a new data collection approach improved the study's reliability.*

**Pair 4:**
- ❌ Before: *We concluded that further research is needed.*
- ✅ After: ***The conclusion** that further research is required was shared by all reviewers.*

### When to USE Nominalization
- ✅ Academic essays, dissertations, research papers
- ✅ Formal reports, executive summaries, policy documents
- ✅ Professional emails and business writing
- ✅ When you want to emphasize the concept rather than the action

### When to AVOID Nominalization
- ❌ Informal writing, personal blogs, casual emails
- ❌ When clarity suffers (too many nouns in one sentence)
- ❌ When the verb form is clearer and more direct
- ❌ Creative or narrative writing

## 2.2 Academic Hedging

### What Is Hedging?

**Hedging** (linguagem de cautela acadêmica) is the use of specific words and phrases to express uncertainty, caution, or possibility rather than stating something as an absolute fact. In academic writing, hedging shows intellectual honesty and careful reasoning.

> **Why hedge?** Because in academic writing, most claims are based on evidence that is not 100% conclusive. Absolute statements ("X always causes Y") are often inaccurate and invite criticism.

---

### Four Categories of Hedging Expressions

#### Category 1: Probability / Possibility
Express that something is likely but not certain.

| Expression | Example Sentence |
|---|---|
| It appears that... | It appears that increased screen time correlates with reduced attention spans. |
| It seems likely that... | It seems likely that economic inequality contributes to social instability. |
| This may suggest... | This may suggest that current policies are insufficient. |
| It could be argued that... | It could be argued that standardized testing disadvantages certain student groups. |
| There is a possibility that... | There is a possibility that the results were affected by confounding variables. |
| might / may / could | The findings might indicate a need for further investigation. |

#### Category 2: Attribution (Reporting What Others Say)
Attribute claims to sources rather than stating them directly.

| Expression | Example Sentence |
|---|---|
| According to... | According to Smith (2019), digital literacy is a core 21st-century skill. |
| Research indicates that... | Research indicates that bilingual children develop stronger cognitive flexibility. |
| The data suggests... | The data suggests a positive correlation between exercise and academic performance. |
| Studies have shown that... | Studies have shown that spaced repetition improves long-term retention. |
| As noted by... | As noted by the WHO (2020), mental health services remain underfunded globally. |

#### Category 3: Frequency / Generalization
Limit the scope of your claim rather than making sweeping generalizations.

| Expression | Example Sentence |
|---|---|
| In most cases... | In most cases, students benefit from structured feedback. |
| This is often seen when... | This is often seen when participants lack prior exposure to the topic. |
| Generally speaking... | Generally speaking, urban populations have greater access to healthcare. |
| Typically... | Typically, the intervention group showed greater improvement. |
| tends to... | This phenomenon tends to occur in low-resource environments. |

#### Category 4: Approximation
Avoid overly precise claims when data is approximate.

| Expression | Example Sentence |
|---|---|
| approximately | The study included approximately 300 participants. |
| roughly | Roughly half of the respondents reported feeling stressed. |
| around | Around 70% of the sample consisted of first-generation university students. |
| nearly | Nearly all participants completed the final assessment. |
| up to | Up to 40% of cases may go unreported due to stigma. |

---

### Comparison: Strong Claim vs. Hedged Version

**Example 1:**
- 🔴 Strong (avoid): *Social media causes depression in teenagers.*
- 🟢 Hedged: *It could be argued that heavy social media use **may** contribute to symptoms of depression in some adolescents, though further research is needed.*
- **Why?** The original makes an absolute causal claim without acknowledging complexity.

**Example 2:**
- 🔴 Strong (avoid): *Online learning is better than traditional classroom instruction.*
- 🟢 Hedged: *Research **suggests** that online learning **tends to** be more effective for self-directed learners, though results **vary significantly** depending on context.*
- **Why?** The hedged version acknowledges variability and avoids over-generalization.

**Example 3:**
- 🔴 Strong (avoid): *This proves that investment in education reduces crime.*
- 🟢 Hedged: *The findings **appear to** indicate that increased educational investment **may** be associated with lower crime rates, though a direct causal link has not been conclusively established.*
- **Why?** Correlation does not equal causation — hedging signals this important distinction.

## 2.3 Academic Paragraph Structure

A well-written academic paragraph has **four essential parts**. Each part has a specific job.

---

### Part 1: Topic Sentence (TS)

**What it is:** The first sentence of the paragraph. It states the main idea or controlling claim of the entire paragraph.

**What it must do:**
- Introduce ONE clear, focused idea
- Connect to the overall thesis of the essay
- Be specific enough to be developed in the paragraph

**Common Mistakes:**
- ❌ Too broad: *"Many things affect student performance."*
- ❌ Too vague: *"This paragraph is about education."*
- ✅ Correct: *"Access to technology in the classroom has been shown to significantly improve student engagement."*

---

### Part 2: Evidence (E)

**What it is:** Facts, data, statistics, quotations, or examples that support the topic sentence.

**Types of Evidence:**
- Empirical data (statistics, experimental results)
- Quotations from experts or primary sources
- Case studies and real-world examples
- Historical examples

**How to Introduce Evidence:**
- *According to [source]...*
- *For example, / For instance,...*
- *Research conducted by [author] found that...*
- *The data indicates that...*
- *As noted by...*

---

### Part 3: Analysis (A)

**What it is:** Your explanation of how the evidence supports the topic sentence. This is where YOUR thinking appears.

**How to connect evidence to your claim:**
- Explain *what* the evidence means
- Explain *why* it matters
- Connect it back to the topic sentence

**Useful Analytical Language:**
- *This suggests that...*
- *This demonstrates / illustrates that...*
- *This is significant because...*
- *Therefore, it can be inferred that...*
- *This finding supports the claim that...*

---

### Part 4: Concluding Sentence (C)

**What it is:** The final sentence of the paragraph. It wraps up the paragraph's idea and sometimes transitions to the next paragraph.

**How to wrap up:**
- Restate the topic sentence idea (in different words)
- Draw a brief conclusion from the evidence
- Use a transition phrase to move forward

**Useful Transition Words:**
- *In conclusion, / To conclude,*
- *Therefore, / Thus,*
- *Ultimately,*
- *Consequently,*
- *As a result,*

---

### Fully Annotated Sample Paragraph

> **[TS]** Critical thinking skills are increasingly recognized as essential for academic success at university level.
> **[E]** A study conducted by Halpern (2014) found that students who received explicit instruction in critical thinking outperformed their peers on complex problem-solving tasks by approximately 35%.
> **[E]** Furthermore, according to the Association of American Colleges and Universities (2018), over 90% of employers rate critical thinking as a "very important" skill in new graduates.
> **[A]** These findings suggest that critical thinking is not merely an academic ability, but a professional competency that bridges classroom learning and workplace performance.
> **[A]** It could therefore be argued that universities have a responsibility to embed critical thinking instruction across all disciplines, rather than treating it as the exclusive domain of philosophy or law.
> **[C]** Ultimately, the development of critical thinking skills appears to be one of the most significant contributions higher education can make to both individual and societal well-being.

## 2.4 Passive Voice in Formal and Scientific Writing

### Formation Table: Active vs. Passive

| Tense | Active Form | Passive Form |
|---|---|---|
| **Present Simple** | Researchers collect the data. | The data **is collected** by researchers. |
| **Past Simple** | The team conducted the study. | The study **was conducted** by the team. |
| **Future Simple** | Experts will present the findings. | The findings **will be presented** by experts. |
| **Present Perfect** | Scientists have discovered a new compound. | A new compound **has been discovered**. |
| **Past Perfect** | They had analyzed the results before publishing. | The results **had been analyzed** before publication. |

> **Formula:** Subject + **to be** (conjugated) + **past participle** + (by + agent — optional)

---

### 3 Rules for Using Passive in Academic Writing

**Rule 1: Use passive when the agent (doer) is unknown or unimportant.**
- The focus is on the action or result, not on who did it.
- ✅ Example: *The samples **were collected** in January and stored at -20°C.*
- (Who collected them is less important than the fact that they were collected.)

**Rule 2: Use passive to create objectivity and distance.**
- Academic writing avoids "I" where possible. Passive removes the writer from the sentence.
- ✅ Example: *It **has been argued** that current economic models fail to account for environmental costs.*
- (Rather than: "I argue that...")

**Rule 3: Use passive when the result or object is the focus of the sentence.**
- Put the most important information in the subject position.
- ✅ Example: *Over 500 participants **were recruited** from three different universities.*
- (The participants are the focus, not the researchers who recruited them.)

---

### 5 Active → Passive Transformation Examples in Academic Sentences

**1.**
- Active: *The committee approved the proposal in 2021.*
- Passive: *The proposal **was approved** by the committee in 2021.*

**2.**
- Active: *Researchers have replicated these findings in six separate studies.*
- Passive: *These findings **have been replicated** in six separate studies.*

**3.**
- Active: *The university will publish the results next semester.*
- Passive: *The results **will be published** next semester.*

**4.**
- Active: *They had already identified several significant limitations before the peer review.*
- Passive: *Several significant limitations **had already been identified** before the peer review.*

**5.**
- Active: *Scientists use this method to measure carbon dioxide levels in the atmosphere.*
- Passive: *This method **is used** to measure carbon dioxide levels in the atmosphere.*

# SECTION 3: 📖 Extended Reading — Academic Writing in Context

## The Role of Critical Thinking in Higher Education

Higher education institutions are increasingly expected to cultivate students' capacity to **analyze**, **evaluate**, and **synthesize** information across complex and rapidly changing knowledge domains. The central **thesis** of much contemporary pedagogical research is that critical thinking — defined as the systematic **analysis** of claims, assumptions, and **evidence** — is not a supplementary skill but a foundational academic competency. According to studies conducted at leading research universities, students who are explicitly trained to **cite** credible sources, **paraphrase** complex texts accurately, and **summarize** key findings without distortion demonstrate significantly stronger academic outcomes than those who rely on rote memorization alone. It could be argued that this disparity reflects a deeper **argument** in educational philosophy: that understanding *how* knowledge is constructed is as important as knowing *what* has been established. A common **counterargument** holds that subject-specific content knowledge should take priority over transferable skills such as critical thinking. However, this position appears to underestimate the degree to which the ability to **synthesize** competing theoretical frameworks is itself a form of advanced content mastery. The **evaluation** of sources, the construction of reasoned arguments, and the integration of multiple disciplinary perspectives have been shown to enhance not only academic performance but also professional readiness. It seems likely, therefore, that the systematic development of critical thinking skills will remain among the most valued outcomes of a university education in the twenty-first century.

## 4.2 Top 10 Hedging Expressions

1. **It appears that...** → *It appears that access to technology improves learning outcomes.*
2. **It could be argued that...** → *It could be argued that traditional methods are no longer sufficient.*
3. **Research suggests that...** → *Research suggests that sleep deprivation affects academic performance.*
4. **According to [source]...** → *According to WHO data, mental health conditions are on the rise.*
5. **It seems likely that...** → *It seems likely that further studies will confirm these findings.*
6. **This may indicate that...** → *This may indicate a need for policy reform.*
7. **In most cases...** → *In most cases, early intervention produces better outcomes.*
8. **There is a possibility that...** → *There is a possibility that the results were affected by bias.*
9. **Generally speaking...** → *Generally speaking, urban populations have better access to services.*

## 4.4 Passive Voice Quick Guide

| Tense | Formula | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Present Simple | is/are + past participle | *The data **is analyzed** weekly.* |
| Past Simple | was/were + past participle | *The study **was conducted** in 2022.* |
| Future Simple | will be + past participle | *The results **will be published** next year.* |
| Present Perfect | has/have been + past participle | *A new method **has been developed**.* |
| Past Perfect | had been + past participle | *The samples **had been stored** correctly.* |

> 💡 **Remember:** Use passive in academic writing to: (1) focus on the result, not the doer; (2) create objectivity; (3) avoid overusing "I" or "we."

## 4.5 Five Rules of Academic Writing (B2 Level)

1. **Be specific, not vague.**
   Avoid broad generalizations. Use data, examples, and precise language to support every claim.
   > ❌ "Many people think education is important."
   > ✅ "Research consistently indicates that tertiary education is associated with higher lifetime earnings and improved health outcomes."

2. **Use hedging language to be intellectually honest.**
   Do not state as fact what is only probable. Use phrases like *it appears that*, *research suggests*, and *it could be argued that* to signal uncertainty.

3. **Structure every paragraph: TS → E → A → C.**
   Every academic paragraph needs a clear topic sentence, supporting evidence, analytical commentary, and a concluding idea. Never leave evidence without analysis.

4. **Cite your sources accurately and consistently.**
   Every idea, statistic, or quote borrowed from another source must be cited. Failing to do so is plagiarism. Use a consistent referencing style (APA, MLA, or Chicago, as required).

5. **Revise for formality and nominalization.**
   After drafting, review your writing for informal language. Replace casual verbs with nominalized forms, remove contractions (don't → do not), and use formal vocabulary appropriate for academic register.
