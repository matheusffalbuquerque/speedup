### Grammar in the Wild

**Instructions:**

## Slide 3: Key Vocabulary — Part 1 📚
### Language for Talking About Language

| Word | Translation | Example Sentence |
|------|-------------|------------------|
| **Syntax** (noun) | Sintaxe | The **syntax** of this sentence is unusual but completely correct. |
| **Nuance** (noun) | Nuance / Sutileza | There is an important **nuance** between "tired" and "exhausted." |
| **Register** (noun) | Registro (formal/informal) | Formal writing requires a different **register** than casual conversation. |
| **Implication** (noun) | Implicação / Subtexto | The **implication** of his silence was that he disagreed. |
| **Coherence** (noun) | Coerência | Good writing requires both grammar accuracy and **coherence** of ideas. |

**Usage Tip:**  
- **Register** refers to the level of formality in language — academic, professional, casual, or literary.
- **Nuance** and **implication** are key words for B2+ discussions about meaning.

## Slide 4: Key Vocabulary — Part 2 📚

| Word | Translation | Example Sentence |
|------|-------------|------------------|
| **Precise** (adj.) | Preciso / Exato | Choose a **precise** word rather than a vague one to avoid ambiguity. |
| **Ambiguous** (adj.) | Ambíguo | The instruction was so **ambiguous** that nobody knew what to do. |
| **Convey** (verb) | Transmitir / Expressar | His tone of voice **conveyed** more than his actual words did. |
| **Elaborate** (verb) | Elaborar / Detalhar | Could you **elaborate** on that point? I'd like to understand better. |

**Word Family Notes:**
- **Precise** → Precisely (adv.) → Precision (noun) → Imprecise (antonym)
- **Ambiguous** → Ambiguously (adv.) → Ambiguity (noun) → Unambiguous (antonym)
- **Convey** → Conveyance (noun) → often used for tone, emotion, and meaning

**[Teacher: Ask students: "Can you think of a situation when someone said something ambiguous? What was the implication?"]**

## Slide 5: Structure 1 — Mixed Conditionals 🔀
### Mixing Time Frames in Conditionals

**What are Mixed Conditionals?**  
Unlike standard conditionals (all in the same time), **mixed conditionals** combine **different time frames** to show how a past situation affects the present, or how a present situation would have changed the past.

**Two main types:**

| Type | Time Frame | Meaning |
|------|------------|---------|
| **Type A** | Past condition → Present result | "If something had happened in the past, things would be different now." |
| **Type B** | Present condition → Past result | "If something were different now, something else would have happened in the past." |

---

## Slide 6: Mixed Conditional — Type A 📌
### Past Condition → Present Result

**Structure:**
> **If + Past Perfect** (had + past participle) **→ would + base verb** (now)

**Examples:**
- If she **had studied** medicine, she **would be** a doctor now.
- If he **hadn't missed** that flight, he **wouldn't be** here with us today.
- If they **had invested** wisely ten years ago, they **would have** a lot more money now.

**Timeline Visual:**
```
[Past Action - hypothetical] ──────────────→ [Present result - hypothetical]
   "had studied"                                "would be a doctor NOW"
```

**Common Errors:**
- ❌ If she studied medicine, she would be a doctor now. *(Standard 2nd conditional — not mixed)*
- ✅ If she **had studied** medicine, she **would be** a doctor now. *(Mixed — past action, present result)*

**[Teacher: Ask students to think of a real-life example: "If I had chosen differently at some point, my life would be _____ now."]**

---

## Slide 7: Mixed Conditional — Type B 📌
### Present Condition → Past Result

**Structure:**
> **If + Past Simple (were / weren't)** → **would have + past participle**

**Examples:**
- If she **were** more organised, she **would have finished** the project on time.
- If he **weren't** so stubborn, he **would have listened** to our advice.
- If I **were** a native speaker, I **would have understood** that joke immediately.

**Timeline Visual:**
```
[Present state - hypothetical] ──────────────→ [Past result - hypothetical]
   "were more organised (NOW)"                  "would have finished (THEN)"
```

**Contrast with Type A:**

| Type A | Type B |
|--------|--------|
| Past cause → Present effect | Present cause → Past effect |
| "If she **had studied**... she **would be**..." | "If she **were** organised... she **would have finished**..." |

**[Teacher: Highlight the direction of time — this is the key challenge for students at B2.]**

## Slide 8: Structure 2 — Inversion for Emphasis 🔁
### Formal Emphasis Through Inverted Word Order

**What is Inversion?**  
Inversion places a **negative or restrictive adverb at the beginning** of the sentence, followed by **auxiliary + subject** (like a question). This creates strong emphasis and is common in formal writing and speeches.

**Key Expressions:**

| Expression | Structure | Example |
|------------|-----------|---------|
| **Never** | Never + aux + subject + verb | **Never have I** seen such dedication. |
| **Not only** | Not only + aux + subject + verb | **Not only did she** pass — she got top marks! |
| **Rarely** | Rarely + aux + subject + verb | **Rarely does he** make such a mistake. |
| **Seldom** | Seldom + aux + subject + verb | **Seldom have we** faced a challenge like this. |
| **Hardly... when** | Hardly + aux + subject + verb + when | **Hardly had I** sat down **when** the phone rang. |
| **No sooner... than** | No sooner + aux + subject + verb + than | **No sooner had she** left **than** it started raining. |

**Standard vs. Inverted (same meaning, different emphasis):**
- Standard: *I have never seen such a beautiful sunset.*
- Inverted: **Never have I seen** such a beautiful sunset.

**[Teacher: Read the inverted versions aloud to demonstrate the dramatic, emphatic tone. Ask students which version sounds more "powerful" or "formal."]**

## Slide 9: Structure 3 — Cleft Sentences for Emphasis ✂️
### Isolating and Highlighting Information

**What are Cleft Sentences?**  
A **cleft sentence** splits one piece of information into two clauses to put emphasis on a specific part. We "cleave" (split) a simple sentence to highlight what matters most.

**Type 1: "It was/is... who/that..."**

| Simple | Cleft (emphasis on subject) |
|--------|-----------------------------|
| John broke the window. | **It was John who** broke the window. *(not someone else)* |
| The noise woke me up. | **It was the noise that** woke me up. *(not something else)* |
| She called me yesterday. | **It was yesterday that** she called me. *(not another time)* |

**Type 2: "What... is/was..."**

| Simple | Cleft (emphasis on need/want/problem) |
|--------|---------------------------------------|
| I need more time. | **What I need is** more time. |
| She wants recognition. | **What she wants is** recognition. |
| Bothers me most is the noise. | **What bothers me most is** the noise. |

**Other Patterns:**
- **The reason why** he left **was that** he was exhausted.
- **The thing that** surprised me most **was** her confidence.
- **All I want is** a little peace and quiet.

**[Teacher: Show students how these structures connect back to the warm-up excerpts. Revisit the board observations from Slide 2!]**

## Slide 10: Guided Practice — Part 1 ✍️
### Mixed Conditionals

**Complete the sentences to form mixed conditionals:**

1. If she ____________ (take) that scholarship, she ____________ (be) living in London right now.

2. If he ____________ (not / be) so impulsive by nature, he ____________ (not / quit) his job so abruptly.

3. They ____________ (have) a better relationship today if they ____________ (communicate) more honestly back then.

4. If I ____________ (be) a more experienced driver, I ____________ (not / panic) during that road test.

5. If we ____________ (invest) in solar panels three years ago, we ____________ (save) a lot of money by now.

**[Teacher: Do the first two together. Then let students attempt 3–5 in pairs before reviewing answers. Discuss why each type is Type A or Type B.]**

## Slide 12: Speaking Activity 🎤
### Grammar in Action — Real Discussions

**Instructions:** In small groups, discuss the questions below. Use **at least one mixed conditional, one inversion, and one cleft sentence** in your discussion.

---

**Topic 1: Life Choices**
> "Think of one decision in your life — big or small. How might your life be different today if you had made a different choice?"

*Tip: Use a Mixed Conditional Type A → "If I had..., I would be... now."*

---

**Topic 2: A Surprising Experience**
> "Tell the group about something that really surprised, impressed, or shocked you."

*Tip: Use Inversion → "Never have I...", "Rarely did I expect..."*

---

**Topic 3: What Really Matters**
> "What is the one thing you wish more people understood about learning a language?"

*Tip: Use a Cleft → "What I believe is...", "It is the practice that..."*

---

**Challenge:** Can you use all three structures in a single, connected paragraph? Try it! 🏆

**[Teacher: After the speaking activity, give brief individual feedback on structure use. Celebrate successful use of advanced grammar!]**
