## Slide 2: Warm-Up Activity 🎯
### Idiom Pictionary

**Objective:** Activate prior knowledge of idioms, reduce speaking anxiety, and generate curiosity about the lesson's vocabulary.

---

#### 🖊️ Materials Needed
- Whiteboard or large sheets of paper (one per student/pair)
- Markers or pens
- Timer (phone or clock)
- Optional: printed idiom cards (teacher cuts and folds the 7 prompts below)

---

#### ⏱️ Suggested Duration: 8–10 minutes

---

#### 📋 Step-by-Step Dynamics

**Step 1 — Set the Scene (1 min)**
Tell students: *"We're going to play Pictionary — but with a twist. Instead of drawing regular words, you'll draw English idioms. Don't write any words or letters. Just draw!"*

**Step 2 — Demonstrate (1 min)**
Draw a quick example on the board. Suggested: *"It's raining cats and dogs"* — sketch rain + a cat and a dog falling. Let students guess to warm up.

**Step 3 — Split into Groups (30 sec)**
- With **2–3 students:** Play as one group (one draws, others guess).
- With **4–5 students:** Split into two pairs; one student rotates as judge/timer.

**Step 4 — Play (5–6 min)**
- Each student picks a folded card (or teacher whispers the idiom).
- Student has **30–45 seconds** to draw; team guesses.
- If no one guesses, the artist explains the idiom in English.
- Rotate until all 7 idioms are used or time is up.

**Step 5 — Bridge to Lesson (1 min)**
Ask: *"Which idiom was the hardest to draw? Why?"* → Use answers to introduce today's lesson.

---

#### 🃏 Idioms for the Game (7 prompts)

| # | Idiom | Drawing Hint |
|---|-------|-------------|
| 1 | **Bite the bullet** | Person biting a bullet, grimacing |
| 2 | **Face the music** | Person facing a loudspeaker/band, looking nervous |
| 3 | **Get the ball rolling** | Person pushing a large rolling ball |
| 4 | **Under the weather** | Small person standing under a rain cloud |
| 5 | **Go the extra mile** | Person running past a finish line with a sign "1 mile" → keeps going |
| 6 | **Break the ice** | Person breaking a large ice block at a party |
| 7 | **Cut corners** | Person cutting the corners of a square with scissors |

---

*[Teacher: This warm-up works even with just 2 students — simply take turns drawing. Encourage students to guess out loud in English and ask "What do you think it means?" before revealing the answer. Avoid explaining the meanings now; let them remain curious. The explanations come in Slides 3–4. If students already know some idioms, celebrate that knowledge and ask them for a sentence using the idiom.]*

## Slide 3: Key Vocabulary — Part 1 📚
### Core Idioms (1–4)

---

### 1. 🔫 Bite the Bullet
> **Tradução:** Aguentar firme / Encarar algo difícil com coragem

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **Register** | Informal / Semi-formal |
| **Context** | Work, medical situations, tough decisions |
| **Example** | *"I didn't want to have the surgery, but I decided to **bite the bullet** and schedule it."* |

💡 **Origin Note:** From 19th-century battlefield medicine — soldiers would literally bite a bullet during surgery without anesthesia to endure the pain.

---

### 2. 🎵 Face the Music
> **Tradução:** Enfrentar as consequências / Assumir a responsabilidade

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **Register** | Informal / Coloquial |
| **Context** | Work mistakes, personal accountability, bad news |
| **Example** | *"He knew he had cut corners on the report — it was time to **face the music** with his manager."* |

💡 **Curiosity:** Possibly from theatre — an actor had to face the orchestra pit (the "music") when coming on stage, regardless of nerves.

---

### 3. ⚽ Get the Ball Rolling
> **Tradução:** Dar início a algo / Colocar as coisas em movimento

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **Register** | Informal / Business-friendly |
| **Context** | Projects, meetings, initiatives |
| **Example** | *"Let's **get the ball rolling** on the new campaign — who wants to present first?"* |

💡 **Tip:** Very common in professional settings. It signals initiative and leadership.

---

### 4. 🌧️ Under the Weather
> **Tradução:** Indisposto / Sentindo-se mal

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **Register** | Informal / Coloquial |
| **Context** | Health, explaining absences, casual conversation |
| **Example** | *"I've been a bit **under the weather** lately — I think I'm coming down with a cold."* |

💡 **Note:** A very polite and indirect way to say you're not feeling well. Preferred in both social and professional spoken English.

---

*[Teacher: Read each idiom aloud and have students repeat. Emphasize pronunciation stress: "BITE the bullet", "face the MU-sic". Ask concept-checking questions: "If your friend says they're under the weather, should you invite them to a party?" to check comprehension. Spend approximately 4–5 minutes on this slide.]*

## Slide 4: Key Vocabulary — Part 2 📚
### Core Idioms (5–7)

---

### 5. 🏃 Go the Extra Mile
> **Tradução:** Fazer mais do que o esperado / Ir além

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **Register** | Semi-formal / Professional |
| **Context** | Work performance, customer service, relationships |
| **Example** | *"Our team always **goes the extra mile** for our clients — that's why they keep coming back."* |

💡 **Biblical Origin:** Comes from the Sermon on the Mount (Matthew 5:41) — "If someone forces you to go one mile, go with them two miles." Now widely used in professional contexts.

---

### 6. 🧊 Break the Ice
> **Tradução:** Quebrar o gelo / Iniciar uma conversa descontraída

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **Register** | Informal / Semi-formal |
| **Context** | Social events, first meetings, introductions, team building |
| **Example** | *"The facilitator played a quick game to **break the ice** before the conference started."* |

💡 **Historical Origin:** From icebreaker ships that would break frozen waterways to allow trade ships to pass — creating a path forward.

---

### 7. ✂️ Cut Corners
> **Tradução:** Fazer algo de forma atabalhoada / Economizar esforço de forma inadequada

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **Register** | Informal / Critical tone |
| **Context** | Quality concerns, construction, academic work, business |
| **Example** | *"The contractor **cut corners** on the building materials, and now the entire project needs to be redone."* |

💡 **Usage Warning:** This idiom almost always carries a **negative connotation** — it implies someone sacrificed quality or ethics to save time or money. Use carefully!

---

#### 🔄 Quick Review — All 7 Idioms

| Idiom | Portuguese Equivalent | Register |
|-------|-----------------------|----------|
| Bite the bullet | Aguentar firme | Informal |
| Face the music | Enfrentar as consequências | Informal |
| Get the ball rolling | Dar início a algo | Semi-formal |
| Under the weather | Sentindo-se mal | Informal |
| Go the extra mile | Ir além do esperado | Semi-formal |
| Break the ice | Quebrar o gelo | Informal |
| Cut corners | Fazer corpo mole / pegar atalho errado | Informal (negative) |

---

*[Teacher: After presenting idiom 7, do a rapid-fire review: point to a student and say the Portuguese equivalent — they say the idiom in English. This reinforces recall. You can also ask "Have you ever cut corners on something? Tell me about it." to generate brief spontaneous speaking practice.]*

## Slide 5: Phrasal Verbs — Separable vs. Inseparable 🔀
### Rules, Examples & Comparison Table

---

### 📌 The Core Rule

| Type | What it means | Pronoun Rule |
|------|---------------|--------------|
| **Separable** | Object can go *between* verb and particle, OR after the full phrasal verb | With pronouns: **MUST** go in the middle |
| **Inseparable** | Object always stays *after* the full phrasal verb | Pronoun stays after the particle — never splits |

---

### ✅ Separable Phrasal Verbs — Position Rules

**Rule for separable verbs:**
- **With a noun:** Object can go before OR after the particle.
  - ✅ *"She **brought up** the topic."*
  - ✅ *"She **brought** the topic **up**."*
- **With a pronoun:** Object MUST go between verb and particle.
  - ✅ *"She **brought it up**."*
  - ❌ *"She brought up it."* ← INCORRECT

---

#### 📋 Separable Phrasal Verbs — Examples

| Phrasal Verb | Meaning (PT) | With Noun | With Pronoun |
|--------------|-------------|-----------|--------------|
| **bring up** | mencionar / criar (filho) | *I brought up the issue.* | *I brought it up.* |
| **turn down** | recusar / diminuir o volume | *She turned down the offer.* | *She turned it down.* |
| **put off** | adiar | *He put off the meeting.* | *He put it off.* |
| **give up** | desistir de | *Don't give up the plan.* | *Don't give it up.* |
| **take on** | assumir / contratar | *We took on the challenge.* | *We took it on.* |
| **back up** | apoiar / fazer backup | *Please back up your data.* | *Please back it up.* |

---

### 🚫 Inseparable Phrasal Verbs — Position Rules

**Rule for inseparable verbs:**
- The object always comes **after** the complete phrasal verb — even when it's a pronoun.
  - ✅ *"She **looked into** the matter."*
  - ✅ *"She **looked into it**."*
  - ❌ *"She looked it into."* ← INCORRECT

---

#### 📋 Inseparable Phrasal Verbs — Examples

| Phrasal Verb | Meaning (PT) | Correct Form | Incorrect Form |
|--------------|-------------|--------------|----------------|
| **look into** | investigar / analisar | *Look into it.* | ~~Look it into.~~ |
| **run into** | encontrar por acaso | *I ran into her yesterday.* | ~~I ran her into.~~ |
| **come across** | encontrar / parecer | *I came across this article.* | ~~I came this across.~~ |
| **figure out** | descobrir / entender | *Figure out the answer.* | ~~Figure the answer out.~~ *(for insep.)* |

---

### 💡 Memory Tip

> **Separable = Flexible** (like opening a sandwich — you can put things inside)
> **Inseparable = Fixed** (like a glued sandwich — you cannot open it)

---

*[Teacher: This is a common source of confusion at B2 level. Write "I brought it up" and "I brought up it" on the board and ask students to identify which is correct before explaining. The pronoun rule is the key diagnostic. After this slide, test them with a quick oral drill: say a phrasal verb + pronoun and have students give the correct word order. E.g., "Turn down / the offer / with a pronoun" → students say "turn it down."]*

## Slide 6: Idioms Across Different Registers 🎭
### Formal, Informal & Colloquial Contexts

---

### 📌 Why Register Matters

Using *break the ice* in a formal business letter or *pursuant to* in a WhatsApp message signals a **mismatch of register** — one of the most common errors at B2+ level. The ability to switch registers appropriately is a mark of true fluency.

---

### 🔄 Same Meaning — Three Different Registers

#### Situation: You want to start a difficult conversation

| Register | Expression | Context |
|----------|-----------|---------|
| **Formal (Written)** | *"I would like to raise the issue of..."* | Professional email, report |
| **Semi-formal (Spoken)** | *"I think it's time we got the ball rolling on this."* | Team meeting, work conversation |
| **Informal/Colloquial** | *"OK, let's just break the ice — we need to talk about what happened."* | Friends, close colleagues |

---

#### Situation: You need to do something unpleasant

| Register | Expression | Context |
|----------|-----------|---------|
| **Formal** | *"We must accept the consequences of our decision."* | Report, official letter |
| **Semi-formal** | *"I'm going to bite the bullet and address this directly."* | Work meeting, professional email |
| **Colloquial** | *"Ugh, I just gotta face the music — I messed up and I know it."* | Text message, casual conversation |

---

### 📨 Real-World Examples

**📧 Professional Email (Semi-formal)**
> *"Following our recent project delays, I think we should go the extra mile in our next client presentation to rebuild trust. I would suggest we bite the bullet and schedule an in-person meeting rather than another video call."*

**💬 Chat Message (Informal/Colloquial)**
> *"Hey! I've been under the weather all week 😷 but I'm finally feeling better. Let's break the ice with the new team this Friday? I'll get the ball rolling and book a table 🎉"*

**📋 Performance Review (Formal-ish)**
> *"The team consistently goes the extra mile to deliver results. It should be noted, however, that certain quality concerns suggest corners were cut during the Q3 rollout, which warrants a review."*

---

### ⚠️ Common Register Errors

| ❌ Wrong Register | ✅ Corrected |
|-------------------|-------------|
| "Dear Sir, I'm under the weather today." | "Dear Sir, I am feeling unwell today and will be unable to attend." |
| "Hey, we gotta face the music ASAP re: the budget." | "Hey, we need to address the budget issue soon." |
| "The client was, like, really going the extra mile, you know?" | "The client was notably committed, going well beyond expectations." |

---

*[Teacher: Ask students to rewrite one of the example sentences by changing its register. E.g., "Take the professional email and make it sound like a WhatsApp message." This is great practice for the IELTS/TOEFL writing tasks where register awareness is assessed. Spend 5–6 minutes and encourage discussion: "Would you use 'bite the bullet' in a job interview?" — there's no single right answer; the discussion IS the learning.]*

## Slide 7: Figures of Speech in English 🖼️
### Metaphor, Hyperbole, Irony & Native Catch Phrases

---

### 1. 🌿 Metaphor (Metáfora)

A **metaphor** describes something *as if* it were something else — without using "like" or "as."

> *"Life is a journey."* (não é uma comparação — é uma afirmação figurativa)
> *"Time is money."*
> *"The meeting was a battlefield."*

**Idioms are often metaphors:**
> *"Get the ball rolling"* → The "ball" is a metaphor for a project or initiative.
> *"Break the ice"* → The "ice" is a metaphor for social tension.

---

### 2. 📢 Hyperbole (Hipérbole)

**Hyperbole** is a deliberate and dramatic exaggeration for effect — very common in informal English.

> *"I've told you a million times!"* (exagero para enfatizar frustração)
> *"I'm so hungry I could eat a horse."*
> *"He's been gone for ages."* (pode significar apenas 10 minutos)
> *"This bag weighs a ton!"*

⚠️ **Brazilian Context:** Portuguese also uses hyperbole (*"Já falei mil vezes!"*), but English hyperboles can be more deadpan — said without exaggerated emotion, which can confuse learners.

---

### 3. 😏 Irony & Sarcasm (Ironia e Sarcasmo)

**Irony** says the *opposite* of what is meant, relying on tone and context.

> *"Oh great, another Monday."* (said with a flat, tired voice → means the opposite)
> *"Sure, cutting corners on a bridge is a brilliant idea."* (obviously sarcastic)
> *"Yeah, because that always works out so well."* (dismissive sarcasm)

💡 **Cultural Note:** British English uses **more understated irony** than Brazilian Portuguese or American English. What sounds like a genuine compliment in British English may actually be irony. E.g., *"That's quite interesting..."* can mean *"That's really boring."*

---

### 4. 💬 Native Catch Phrases & Everyday Expressions

| Expression | Meaning (PT) | When to Use |
|------------|-------------|-------------|
| *"That's the thing..."* | É o que acontece / Mas veja bem... | To introduce a complication or twist |
| *"At the end of the day..."* | No fim das contas... | To summarize or conclude informally |
| *"It is what it is."* | É o que é. / Não tem jeito. | Accepting an unchangeable situation |
| *"I get where you're coming from."* | Entendo de onde você está vindo. | Showing empathy/understanding |
| *"Fair enough."* | Tudo bem / Tá bom. | Accepting a point or argument |
| *"Been there, done that."* | Já passei por isso. | Expressing been-through experience |
| *"No worries."* | Sem problema / Tudo bem. | Very casual reassurance |
| *"That makes two of us."* | Eu também! / A gente tá junto nessa. | Agreeing with someone's feeling |

---

*[Teacher: The hyperbole section is great for a quick game — say a hyperbole and students rate if it sounds natural ("I could eat a horse") or unnatural ("I could eat 500 horses"). This sharpens their feel for authentic English exaggeration. For irony, use tone of voice: say "Oh, fantastic" twice — once genuinely, once sarcastically — and ask students to identify the difference. This is crucial for IELTS listening and everyday native comprehension.]*

## Slide 10: Speaking Activity 🗣️
### Real-World Scenarios — Idioms & Phrasal Verbs in Action

---

*[Teacher: Divide students into pairs or small groups (adapt for 2–5 students). Each pair receives one scenario card. Allow 3–4 minutes of preparation, then 2–3 minutes of spoken interaction. Rotate scenarios if time allows. Encourage natural use of at least 2 idioms or phrasal verbs per interaction. Observe without interrupting — note errors for a brief feedback session afterwards.]*

---

### 🎭 Scenario 1: The Overdue Conversation
**Context:** You are a team leader. One of your employees has been cutting corners on client deliverables, and a client has complained. You need to call a meeting to address this professionally.

**Task:** Role-play the meeting. The employee must face the music; the leader must get the ball rolling on a solution.

**Target language:** *face the music, cut corners, get the ball rolling, look into, bring up, figure out*

---

### 🎭 Scenario 2: The New Job Jitters
**Context:** Two friends are having coffee. One just started a new job and is feeling under the weather from stress. The other has experience and wants to give advice about how to break the ice with new colleagues and go the extra mile without burning out.

**Task:** Have a natural 3-minute conversation. Use at least 3 idioms from today's lesson.

**Target language:** *under the weather, break the ice, go the extra mile, come across, figure out, bite the bullet*

---

### 🎭 Scenario 3: The Deadline Crisis
**Context:** Two colleagues are messaging (spoken simulation). They've been putting off an important report, and the deadline is tomorrow. One wants to back up all data before starting; the other is panicking but ready to bite the bullet.

**Task:** Simulate the conversation. Discuss what to do, divide tasks, and motivate each other.

**Target language:** *bite the bullet, put off, back up, take on, go the extra mile, get the ball rolling*

---

### 🎭 Scenario 4: The Awkward Party
**Context:** You're at a professional networking event. You don't know anyone. Discuss with a partner how you would use idioms and small talk strategies to break the ice, what to say, how to come across as confident, and how to avoid cutting corners on making a good first impression.

**Task:** One student plays the nervous attendee; the other plays a confident networker giving tips.

**Target language:** *break the ice, come across, run into, go the extra mile, under the weather, that's the thing..., I get where you're coming from*

---

*[Teacher: After each scenario, give 60 seconds of group feedback. Highlight 1-2 excellent uses of idioms and 1 correction. Avoid correcting every error — fluency is the priority in this activity. If the group is advanced, add a challenge: "Redo the scenario but change the register — make it more formal/informal."]*
