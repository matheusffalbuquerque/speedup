### Part 1 — Core Idioms (7)

---

#### 1. Bite the Bullet
> 🇧🇷 **Tradução:** Aguentar firme / Encarar algo difícil com coragem / "Engolir o sapo"

| | |
|---|---|
| **Register** | Informal / Semi-formal |
| **Context** | Tough decisions, medical situations, professional challenges |
| **Collocations** | *just bite the bullet, have to bite the bullet, decide to bite the bullet* |

**Natural Examples:**
- *"I finally bit the bullet and had that difficult conversation with my manager."*
- *"The renovation will be expensive, but we need to bite the bullet and do it properly."*

📝 **Usage Note:** Implies that the action is unpleasant but necessary and unavoidable. Almost always followed by *and + verb*.

---

#### 2. Face the Music
> 🇧🇷 **Tradução:** Enfrentar as consequências / Assumir a responsabilidade

| | |
|---|---|
| **Register** | Informal |
| **Context** | Mistakes, accountability, receiving criticism |
| **Collocations** | *have to face the music, it's time to face the music, eventually face the music* |

**Natural Examples:**
- *"After months of avoiding the problem, she finally had to face the music."*
- *"He knew he had cut corners — sooner or later, he'd have to face the music."*

📝 **Usage Note:** Suggests reluctance before acceptance. Often used when someone has been *avoiding* a situation.

---

#### 3. Get the Ball Rolling
> 🇧🇷 **Tradução:** Dar início a algo / Colocar as coisas em movimento

| | |
|---|---|
| **Register** | Informal / Business-friendly |
| **Context** | Meetings, projects, initiatives, group work |
| **Collocations** | *let's get the ball rolling, get things rolling, keep the ball rolling* |

**Natural Examples:**
- *"I'll get the ball rolling by sharing the project brief."*
- *"Someone needs to get the ball rolling — we've been stalling for two weeks."*

📝 **Usage Note:** Very common in professional settings. Signals leadership and initiative. *"Keep the ball rolling"* means to maintain momentum.

---

#### 4. Under the Weather
> 🇧🇷 **Tradução:** Indisposto / Sentindo-se mal / Não estar bem

| | |
|---|---|
| **Register** | Informal / Coloquial |
| **Context** | Health, explaining absences, casual conversation |
| **Collocations** | *feeling a bit under the weather, been under the weather, a little under the weather* |

**Natural Examples:**
- *"I'm feeling a bit under the weather — I might work from home today."*
- *"She's been under the weather all week; I don't think she'll make it to the event."*

📝 **Usage Note:** A soft, polite way to mention feeling unwell. Preferred in spoken English and casual written communication.

---

#### 5. Go the Extra Mile
> 🇧🇷 **Tradução:** Fazer mais do que o esperado / Ir além / Se superar

| | |
|---|---|
| **Register** | Semi-formal / Professional |
| **Context** | Work performance, customer service, personal relationships |
| **Collocations** | *always go the extra mile, willing to go the extra mile, go the extra mile for someone* |

**Natural Examples:**
- *"Our customer service team goes the extra mile to ensure client satisfaction."*
- *"He went the extra mile by delivering the report two days early."*

📝 **Usage Note:** Almost always carries a **positive connotation**. Common in job interviews, performance reviews, and LinkedIn profiles.

---

#### 6. Break the Ice
> 🇧🇷 **Tradução:** Quebrar o gelo / Iniciar uma conversa descontraída / Aliviar a tensão

| | |
|---|---|
| **Register** | Informal / Semi-formal |
| **Context** | Social events, first meetings, team building, introductions |
| **Collocations** | *break the ice with someone, use something to break the ice, break the ice at a party* |

**Natural Examples:**
- *"The facilitator used a quick game to break the ice before the training began."*
- *"Making a small joke is a great way to break the ice in a new work environment."*

📝 **Usage Note:** Can also be used as a noun: *"an icebreaker activity."* The noun form is more common in professional/educational settings.

---

#### 7. Cut Corners
> 🇧🇷 **Tradução:** Atalhar de forma inadequada / Fazer corpo mole / Economizar esforço à custa da qualidade

| | |
|---|---|
| **Register** | Informal (negative connotation) |
| **Context** | Work quality, construction, academic work, ethics |
| **Collocations** | *cut corners on something, can't afford to cut corners, accused of cutting corners* |

**Natural Examples:**
- *"If you cut corners on the safety inspection, you're putting lives at risk."*
- *"The budget is tight, but we can't afford to cut corners on quality."*

📝 **Usage Note:** ⚠️ Almost always **negative** — implies someone sacrificed quality, safety, or ethics to save time or money. Be careful with this one in professional contexts.

## 🎯 Summary of Linguistic Structures

### Structure 1 — Phrasal Verbs: Separable vs. Inseparable

#### 📌 The Golden Rule for Pronouns

| Type | With Noun (Flexible) | With Pronoun (Fixed) |
|------|---------------------|---------------------|
| **Separable** | Object before OR after particle | Pronoun MUST go BETWEEN verb and particle |
| **Inseparable** | Object always AFTER the particle | Pronoun also AFTER the particle — NEVER splits |

---

#### ✅ Separable — Full Pattern Examples

| Phrasal Verb | With Noun (both OK) | With Pronoun (only correct form) |
|-------------|---------------------|----------------------------------|
| **bring up** | *Bring up the topic* / *Bring the topic up* | ✅ *Bring **it** up* ❌ ~~Bring up it~~ |
| **turn down** | *Turn down the offer* / *Turn the offer down* | ✅ *Turn **it** down* ❌ ~~Turn down it~~ |
| **put off** | *Put off the meeting* / *Put the meeting off* | ✅ *Put **it** off* ❌ ~~Put off it~~ |
| **give up** | *Give up the plan* / *Give the plan up* | ✅ *Give **it** up* ❌ ~~Give up it~~ |
| **take on** | *Take on the project* / *Take the project on* | ✅ *Take **it** on* ❌ ~~Take on it~~ |
| **back up** | *Back up the data* / *Back the data up* | ✅ *Back **it** up* ❌ ~~Back up it~~ |

---

#### 🚫 Inseparable — Full Pattern Examples

| Phrasal Verb | With Noun ✅ | With Pronoun ✅ | Incorrect Form ❌ |
|-------------|------------|---------------|-----------------|
| **look into** | *Look into the matter* | *Look into **it*** | ~~Look **it** into~~ |
| **run into** | *Run into a problem* | *Run into **it*** | ~~Run **it** into~~ |
| **come across** | *Come across an article* | *Come across **it*** | ~~Come **it** across~~ |
| **figure out** | *Figure out the answer* | *Figure **it** out* | *(mostly separable — both used)* |

### Structure 2 — Register Guide for Idioms

#### 📋 When to Use Each Register

| Register | Context | Characteristics | Idiom Usage |
|----------|---------|-----------------|-------------|
| **Formal** | Official emails, reports, presentations, academic writing | No contractions, no colloquialisms, precise vocabulary | Avoid idioms or use very carefully |
| **Semi-formal** | Work conversations, team emails, meetings | Contractions OK, professional but approachable | Idioms like *bite the bullet*, *get the ball rolling* acceptable |
| **Informal** | Friends, family, close colleagues | Contractions, slang, filler words OK | All today's idioms are appropriate |
| **Colloquial** | Text messages, social media, casual speech | Abbreviations, very relaxed grammar | Any idiom + slang freely |

---

#### 🔄 The Same Message — Four Registers

**Situation:** You are not feeling well and need to cancel a commitment.

| Register | Example |
|----------|---------|
| **Formal** | *"I regret to inform you that I am feeling unwell and will be unable to attend today's meeting. I apologise for any inconvenience caused."* |
| **Semi-formal** | *"Hi team — I'm feeling a bit under the weather today and won't be able to join the call. Can we reschedule for tomorrow?"* |
| **Informal** | *"Hey, I'm under the weather today — really not feeling it. Can we push it back?"* |
| **Colloquial** | *"Ugh, feeling awful rn 🤒 gotta skip today, sry! Can we do tmrw?"* |

### Structure 3 — Figures of Speech in English

#### 🌿 Metaphor (Metáfora)
A direct comparison that states one thing *is* another — without "like" or "as."
> *"Life is a rollercoaster."*
> *"The negotiation was a chess match."*
> *"He's a walking encyclopedia."*

**How idioms use metaphor:** Most idioms are frozen metaphors. *"Break the ice"* uses the metaphor of breaking actual ice to represent breaking social tension.

---

#### 📢 Hyperbole (Hipérbole)
Deliberate, dramatic exaggeration used for emphasis or humour.
> *"I've been waiting for ages."* (talvez 5 minutos)
> *"I'm absolutely starving."* (apenas com fome normal)
> *"This bag weighs a ton."* (é apenas pesado)
> *"I told you a thousand times!"*

⚠️ **Key Point:** In English, hyperbole is often delivered with a **flat tone** (especially in British English). Don't expect the speaker to sound dramatic — the exaggeration is in the words, not always the voice.

---

#### 😏 Irony & Sarcasm (Ironia e Sarcasmo)
Saying the opposite of what is meant — relying on context and tone.
> *"Oh great, another traffic jam."* (dito de forma cansada → significa o oposto)
> *"Sure, skipping the safety test is a brilliant plan."* (sarcasmo óbvio)
> *"What a lovely surprise."* (quando claramente não é)

**British vs. American Irony:**
- 🇬🇧 *British:* Often understated, deadpan, subtle. *"That's quite nice."* can mean *"That's really boring."*
- 🇺🇸 *American:* Tends to be more obvious, with audible sarcasm cues.
- 🇧🇷 *Brazilian:* Similar to American — irony is often more clearly marked vocally.

---

### Structure 4 — Native Catch Phrases & Authentic Expressions

| Expression | 🇧🇷 Tradução | When to Use | Example |
|------------|-------------|-------------|---------|
| *"That's the thing..."* | É o que acontece / Mas veja bem... | Introducing a complication or twist | *"I want to go, but that's the thing — I have no money."* |
| *"At the end of the day..."* | No fim das contas | Summarizing, concluding informally | *"At the end of the day, what matters is that we tried."* |
| *"It is what it is."* | É o que é. / Não tem jeito. | Accepting something unchangeable | *"The deadline passed. It is what it is — let's move on."* |
| *"I get where you're coming from."* | Entendo seu ponto de vista | Showing empathy, acknowledging a position | *"I get where you're coming from, but I disagree."* |
| *"Fair enough."* | Tudo bem / Faz sentido | Accepting a reasonable argument | *"You need more time? Fair enough."* |
| *"Been there, done that."* | Já passei por isso | Showing shared/prior experience | *"Job hunting is exhausting. Been there, done that."* |
| *"No worries."* | Sem problema / Tudo bem | Casual reassurance | *"Sorry I'm late!" / "No worries at all."* |
| *"That makes two of us."* | Eu também / A gente tá junto nessa | Agreeing with someone's feeling | *"I have no idea what's going on." / "That makes two of us."* |

#### **The Turning Point**

When Natalia Ferreira accepted the position of Head of Operations at Crestwood Technologies, she knew she was taking on the most demanding challenge of her career. The company had been struggling for two years, and the board made it clear: she had six months to turn things around, or they would restructure the entire department.

In her first week, Natalia decided to get the ball rolling by scheduling individual meetings with each team member. She wanted to understand what had gone wrong before proposing solutions. What she found was disheartening. The previous manager had consistently cut corners on quality control to hit short-term targets, and now the backlog of unresolved technical issues was enormous.

"We need to look into every open complaint — not just the recent ones," she told her team. "I know it's a lot, but we can't afford to keep putting it off."

Some of her colleagues pushed back. Tomás, the senior developer, came across as skeptical at first. "We've been here before," he said flatly. "New managers always bite the bullet for about three weeks and then fall back into the same patterns."

Natalia appreciated his honesty. She understood where he was coming from. *That's the thing about broken systems,* she thought. *People stop trusting the people who promise to fix them.*

Rather than getting defensive, she decided to bite the bullet herself and address the morale problem head-on. In the next team meeting, she didn't give a polished presentation. She stood up and said: "I know trust is earned, not given. I'm not asking you to believe me today. I'm asking you to watch what we do over the next 30 days."

She stayed late. She went the extra mile on every client complaint, personally following up on issues that had been sitting in the queue for months. When she ran into problems that required new resources, she didn't try to work around the budget — she brought them up directly with the board.

Gradually, things began to shift. Tomás, who had been the biggest skeptic, started backing her up in meetings. The atmosphere in the office changed. People stopped looking for shortcuts.

By month four, Crestwood's client retention rate had improved by 22%. The board called it "a remarkable turnaround." But for Natalia, the real victory was something smaller: the morning Tomás walked into her office, put a coffee on her desk, and said, "Fair enough. You were right."

She smiled. At the end of the day, that's what leadership really looked like — not the titles or the metrics, but the moment someone who had stopped believing decided to start again.
