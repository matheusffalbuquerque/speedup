## Slide 3: Key Vocabulary — Part 1 📚
### The Language of Discourse

| Word | Translation | Example Sentence |
|------|-------------|------------------|
| **Cohesion** (noun) | Coesão | The essay lacks **cohesion** — the paragraphs do not connect smoothly. |
| **Coherence** (noun) | Coerência | A text can be grammatically correct but still lack **coherence** of ideas. |
| **Transition** (noun/verb) | Transição / Conectar | Words like *however* and *therefore* serve as **transitions** between ideas. |
| **Paraphrase** (verb/noun) | Parafrasear | Can you **paraphrase** that last point? Some students didn't follow it. |
| **Rephrase** (verb) | Reformular | Let me **rephrase** that — I don't think I expressed my point clearly. |

**Key Distinction:**
- **Cohesion** = the *surface-level* links between sentences (pronouns, connectors, repetition)
- **Coherence** = the *logical flow* and meaning across the whole text
- A text can be cohesive but *not* coherent — and coherent but *not* very cohesive!

**[Teacher: Write both words on the board. Ask: "A text with good grammar but totally random ideas — is it cohesive? Coherent?" (Neither) "A text where every sentence logically follows but without any linking words — is it cohesive? Coherent?" (Coherent but not cohesive). This contrast builds critical metalinguistic awareness.]**

## Slide 4: Key Vocabulary — Part 2 📚

| Word | Translation | Example Sentence |
|------|-------------|------------------|
| **Elaborate** (verb) | Elaborar / Desenvolver | Could you **elaborate** on how you reached that conclusion? |
| **Digress** (verb) | Desviar do assunto | I realise I'm **digressing** — let me return to the main point. |
| **Resume** (verb) | Retomar / Continuar | After the interruption, she **resumed** her argument confidently. |
| **Acknowledge** (verb) | Reconhecer / Admitir | Good academic writers **acknowledge** opposing viewpoints before refuting them. |

**Word Family Notes:**
- **Elaborate** → Elaboration (noun) → Elaborately (adv.) → "an elaborate plan"
- **Digress** → Digression (noun) → "a brief digression" (common in speeches and essays)
- **Resume** → Resumption (noun) ⚠️ *Do not confuse with "résumé" (CV/curriculum)*
- **Acknowledge** → Acknowledgement (noun) → "in acknowledgement of..." (formal)

**Discourse Signals in Real Speech:**
> *"To **elaborate** on that point..."*  
> *"I know I'm **digressing**, but..."*  
> *"**Resuming** my earlier argument..."*  
> *"I **acknowledge** that this view has critics, however..."*

**[Teacher: Ask students: "When might you use 'digress' in a presentation? Have you ever lost track of your main point when speaking English?" — connects vocabulary to real communicative need.]**

## Slide 5: Structure 1 — Advanced Connectors 🔗
### Beyond "However" and "Therefore"

**What are Advanced Connectors?**  
At B2+, relying only on basic connectors (*but, so, because, although*) makes your writing and speech feel elementary. **Advanced connectors** allow you to express more precise logical relationships and signal a higher register.

| Connector | Meaning (PT) | Register | Example Sentence |
|-----------|-------------|----------|-----------------|
| **Notwithstanding** | Apesar disso / Não obstante | Formal / Legal | The risks were significant; **notwithstanding**, the board approved the plan. |
| **Albeit** | Embora / Ainda que | Formal / Literary | She accepted the offer, **albeit** reluctantly. |
| **Inasmuch as** | Na medida em que / Visto que | Formal / Academic | His findings are relevant **inasmuch as** they challenge the existing model. |
| **In that** | No sentido de que / Pois | Formal / Academic | The policy failed **in that** it never addressed the root cause. |
| **To the extent that** | A tal ponto que / Na medida em que | Formal / Academic | The problem has worsened **to the extent that** intervention is now unavoidable. |

**Register Reminder:**
- These connectors are **not** appropriate in casual conversation
- They are ideal in: academic writing, formal presentations, professional reports, opinion essays

**[Teacher: Read each example aloud with appropriate intonation. Ask students to listen for the "weight" these connectors carry vs. simpler alternatives. "The risks were significant; nevertheless..." feels lighter than "...notwithstanding."]**

## Slide 6: Advanced Connectors — Usage in Context 🔗
### Patterns and Traps

**Positioning and Punctuation:**

| Connector | Typical Position | Punctuation Note |
|-----------|-----------------|-----------------|
| **Notwithstanding** | Start of clause OR end of clause | *Notwithstanding,* + clause / noun phrase + *notwithstanding* |
| **Albeit** | Before adjective, adverb, or reduced clause | No comma: *albeit reluctantly* |
| **Inasmuch as** | Middle of sentence | *X inasmuch as Y* — introduces a reason |
| **In that** | Middle of sentence | *X in that Y* — specifies/clarifies X |
| **To the extent that** | Middle of sentence | *X to the extent that Y* — shows consequence/degree |

**Comparing Advanced vs. Basic Connectors (same meaning):**

| Basic | Advanced | Shift in Register |
|-------|----------|------------------|
| Although she was tired, she continued. | She continued, **albeit** exhausted. | Formal, more compact |
| Because it covers all costs, it's fair. | It is fair **inasmuch as** it covers all costs. | Academic, more precise |
| Despite the risks, they proceeded. | **Notwithstanding** the risks, they proceeded. | Formal / Legal |
| It was so bad that we had to leave. | It worsened **to the extent that** we had to leave. | Nuanced degree |

**Common Error to Avoid:**
- ❌ *"Albeit the traffic was heavy, we arrived on time."* ← **albeit** does NOT introduce a full clause
- ✅ *"We arrived on time, **albeit** late by a few minutes."* ← reduced structure only

**[Teacher: Emphasise the albeit error — this is the most frequently misused. Write both versions on the board. Ask students: "Why can't 'albeit' work like 'although'? What is missing?"]**

## Slide 7: Structure 2 — Ellipsis & Substitution ✂️
### Avoiding Unnecessary Repetition

**What is Ellipsis?**  
**Ellipsis** = omitting words that are already understood from context.  
**Substitution** = replacing repeated words with a shorter form (*do, so, one, ones, not*).

Both are essential for **natural, fluent English** — and for **cohesion** without repetition.

---

### ❌ With Repetition (Awkward):

> *"She wanted to leave early. He wanted to leave early too. They had decided to leave early the day before."*

> *"I like action movies. My sister likes action movies as well. We both enjoy watching action movies on weekends."*

---

### ✅ With Ellipsis & Substitution (Fluent):

> *"She wanted to leave early, and he did **too** [= wanted to leave early]. They had decided **to** [= to leave early] the day before."*

> *"I like action movies, and my sister does **too** [= likes them]. We both enjoy watching **them** on weekends."*

---

**Key Substitution Forms:**

| Repeated Element | Substitution | Example |
|-----------------|-------------|---------|
| Verb phrase (+ to) | **do / does / did** | *"She studied abroad." — "Did she? So did I."* |
| Infinitive clause | **to** (alone) | *"I didn't want to quit, but I had **to**."* |
| Countable noun | **one / ones** | *"I need a new pen — this **one** is broken."* |
| Whole clause/idea | **so / not** | *"Will it rain?" — "I think **so** / I hope **not**."* |

**Ellipsis in Speech:**
> *"Coming to the party?" ← [Are you] Coming to the party?*  
> *"Ready?" ← [Are you] Ready?*  
> *"Thought you'd never ask." ← [I] Thought you'd never ask.*

**[Teacher: The speech examples of ellipsis show that native speakers drop subject + auxiliary constantly in informal contexts. Ask: "Have you heard any of these in films or series?" Play a short authentic clip if available.]**

## Slide 8: Structure 3 — Reference Devices 🔍
### Pointing Back and Forward in a Text

**What are Reference Devices?**  
Words that **refer to** something already mentioned (anaphoric) or soon to be mentioned (cataphoric), creating **cohesion** without repetition.

---

**The Key Reference Devices at B2+:**

| Device | Function | Example in Context |
|--------|----------|--------------------|
| **This** | Refers to idea just stated (close / abstract) | *Many employees now work remotely. **This** has changed how teams communicate.* |
| **That** | Refers to a more distant idea or contrasts | *The old system was inefficient. **That** became obvious during the audit.* |
| **Such** | Refers to a previously described quality/type | *The board rejected all informal proposals. **Such** decisions require formal submission.* |
| **The former** | Refers to the FIRST of two things mentioned | *We considered both expansion and consolidation. **The former** was deemed too risky.* |
| **The latter** | Refers to the SECOND of two things mentioned | *Employees preferred flexibility over higher salaries. **The latter** was less of a priority.* |

---

**See them in a real paragraph:**

> *The report outlined two possible strategies: immediate restructuring and phased adaptation.*  
> ***The former** would require significant investment; **the latter**, however, offered a more gradual transition.*  
> ***Such** decisions are rarely straightforward, and **this** is why external consultants were brought in.*  
> ***That** level of uncertainty had not been anticipated by the leadership team.*

**[Teacher: Project the paragraph and ask students to draw arrows from each reference device back to what it refers to. This visual mapping activity makes cohesion concrete. Ask: "What would happen if we replaced all these devices with the original nouns?" → Read it aloud and show how clunky it becomes.]**

## Slide 9: Structure 4 — Hedging & Boosting ⚖️
### Calibrating the Strength of Your Claims

**Why does this matter?**  
In academic, professional, and advanced spoken English, speakers rarely make **absolute claims**. Instead, they **hedge** (soften, qualify) or **boost** (strengthen, affirm) their statements to signal certainty level, intellectual honesty, and rhetorical purpose.

---

### 🛡️ Hedging Expressions — Softening a Claim

*Use when: you are not 100% certain, presenting data, or avoiding overgeneralisation*

| Expression | Example in Use |
|------------|---------------|
| **tends to** | *Remote work **tends to** improve individual productivity, though results vary.* |
| **appears / seems to** | *The economy **appears to** be stabilising, according to recent indicators.* |
| **would seem to suggest** | *The data **would seem to suggest** a correlation, albeit not a causal link.* |
| **it could be argued that** | ***It could be argued that** social media has widened, not narrowed, social divides.* |
| **to some extent** | *The policy has, **to some extent**, achieved its intended outcomes.* |
| **in many / most cases** | ***In most cases**, early intervention leads to better long-term results.* |
| **there is a possibility that** | ***There is a possibility that** the results were influenced by external factors.* |

---

### 🚀 Boosting Expressions — Strengthening a Claim

*Use when: evidence is strong, making an emphatic point, or expressing consensus*

| Expression | Example in Use |
|------------|---------------|
| **undoubtedly** | *This is **undoubtedly** the most significant reform in a decade.* |
| **clearly / evidently** | ***Clearly**, the current approach is no longer sustainable.* |
| **arguably** | *This is **arguably** the most misunderstood concept in modern economics.* |
| **it is widely accepted that** | ***It is widely accepted that** diversity improves team performance.* |
| **there is no question that** | ***There is no question that** the decision had lasting consequences.* |
| **without doubt** | *The initiative was, **without doubt**, a turning point for the organisation.* |

**[Teacher: Point out that "arguably" is a booster — it means "this is a strong position one can defend" — NOT a hedge, despite sounding uncertain. This is a very common confusion at B2 level. Ask: "Which list would you use more in a formal essay? Which in a spoken debate?"]**

### Discourse in Action — Structured Discussion

**Instructions:** In small groups, choose **one topic** below. Each person should speak for at least **60–90 seconds**. The group's goal: use as many discourse tools from today's lesson as possible — naturally, not forced!

---

**Topic 1: Technology & Society**
> *"Has technology made human communication better or worse? Consider both sides."*

*Targets:*
- Use **the former / the latter** to contrast two ideas
- Use **arguably / it could be argued** to present a debatable position
- Use **albeit** or **notwithstanding** to concede a point

---

**Topic 2: Education**
> *"Is a university degree still worth pursuing in today's job market?"*

*Targets:*
- Use **hedging** to avoid overgeneralising (*tends to, in many cases, to some extent*)
- Use **this / such** as reference devices when building your argument
- Use **inasmuch as** to justify a claim

---

**Topic 3: Work & Life Balance**
> *"Remote work has permanently changed the nature of the workplace. Do you agree?"*

*Targets:*
- Use **notwithstanding** to acknowledge a counterargument
- Use **ellipsis or substitution** to avoid repetition as you develop your ideas
- Use **undoubtedly / there is no question that** to make a strong closing point
