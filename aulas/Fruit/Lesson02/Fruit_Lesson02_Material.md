---

**⚠️ Important Notes:**

> **Cohesion vs. Coherence — a critical distinction:**
> - **Cohesion** is about the **surface form** of a text — how sentences are grammatically and lexically linked (connectors, pronouns, reference devices, ellipsis).
> - **Coherence** is about the **logical flow of ideas** — whether the argument makes sense, stays on topic, and builds progressively.
> - A text can be cohesive but incoherent (well-linked sentences that go nowhere), or coherent but poorly cohesive (good logic expressed clumsily). Strong writers aim for both.
>
> *Example:*
> - *Poor cohesion:* "She studied hard. The results were good. Coffee was on the table."
> - *Poor coherence:* "Furthermore, as previously stated, in addition, it was mentioned above that..."

---

> **Digress ↔ Resume — an important pair:**
> - **Digress** = to temporarily move away from the main topic ("Let me just digress for a moment…")
> - **Resume** = to return to the main topic after a digression ("To resume our discussion of…")
> - They work as **opposite poles** in discourse management.
> - ⚠️ Don't confuse **resume** (retomar) with **résumé** (currículo — with accent marks and French origin).

---

> **Acknowledge — multiple uses:**
> - Recognise a fact or reality: *"We must acknowledge that progress has been slow."*
> - Admit someone else's point: *"I acknowledge your concern, but the data tells a different story."*
> - Show awareness of opposing evidence: *"The authors acknowledge the limitations of the study."*
> - In social contexts, to greet/notice someone: *"He walked past without acknowledging her."*

### 1. Advanced Connectors

Advanced connectors allow you to express **complex logical relationships** with precision and formal register. They are common in academic writing, professional reports, editorials, and sophisticated spoken discourse.

---

#### Full Reference Table

| Connector | Portuguese Equivalent | Register | Function / When to Use | Example |
|-----------|----------------------|----------|------------------------|---------|
| **Notwithstanding** | Não obstante / Apesar de | Highly formal / Legal | Concede a point while maintaining a contrasting conclusion. Follows or precedes the conceded point. | **Notwithstanding** the considerable investment, the project yielded disappointing results. |
| **Albeit** | Embora / Ainda que | Formal / Academic | Introduces a mild concession within a sentence, similar to "even though" but more compact. Always followed by an adjective, adverb, or reduced clause. | The proposal was approved, **albeit** reluctantly, by the entire board. |
| **Inasmuch as** | Na medida em que / Visto que | Very formal / Academic | Introduces a reason, justification, or degree. Equivalent to "in so far as" or "because, to the degree that." | The regulation is problematic **inasmuch as** it creates unequal burdens on small businesses. |
| **In that** | No sentido de que / Porque | Formal / Academic | Explains the specific way in which something is true — a precise "because" or "in the sense that." | The approach is innovative **in that** it combines quantitative and qualitative methods. |
| **To the extent that** | Na medida em que / A tal ponto que | Formal | Indicates a degree or limit — how far something applies or is true. | The policy was successful **to the extent that** it reduced unemployment, but it failed to address inflation. |

---

#### Notwithstanding vs. Despite — Key Difference

| Feature | **Notwithstanding** | **Despite** |
|---------|--------------------|-----------:|
| **Register** | Highly formal / Legal | Formal and semi-formal |
| **Position** | Can precede OR follow the noun phrase | Precedes the noun phrase only |
| **Flexibility** | Wider syntactic freedom | More fixed position |
| **Example** | *The risks notwithstanding, they proceeded.* | *Despite the risks, they proceeded.* |
| **Tone** | More authoritative, legalistic | Neutral formal |

> **Rule:** Both take a **noun phrase** or **gerund** — NOT a full clause with subject + verb.
> - ✅ *Notwithstanding the challenges, …*
> - ✅ *Despite facing setbacks, …*
> - ❌ *Despite they faced setbacks, …*

---

#### Formation Rules at a Glance

| Connector | Follows with | Common Error to Avoid |
|-----------|-------------|----------------------|
| **Albeit** | adjective / adverb / reduced clause | ❌ *albeit that she was tired* → ✅ *albeit tired* |
| **Inasmuch as** | full clause (S + V) | Don't use as a one-word transition like "therefore" |
| **In that** | full clause explaining how/why | Don't confuse with "in which" (relative pronoun) |
| **To the extent that** | full clause with degree meaning | Don't confuse with "to the extent of" + gerund |

### 2. Ellipsis & Substitution

These are two powerful grammatical mechanisms for **avoiding repetition** while maintaining clarity — a mark of sophisticated fluency in both writing and speech.

---

#### Definitions

> **Ellipsis** = the *omission* of words that are understood from context.
> *(You simply leave them out.)*
>
> **Substitution** = replacing repeated words with a *pro-form* (a shorter word that "stands in" for the original).
> *(You replace them with something shorter.)*

---

#### Ellipsis — Examples and Forms

| Original (with repetition) | With Ellipsis | Type |
|---------------------------|---------------|------|
| "He wants to leave, and she wants to leave too." | "He wants to leave, and she wants to **[∅]** too." | Infinitive ellipsis |
| "Can you speak Spanish?" / "Yes, I can speak Spanish." | "Yes, I **can [∅]**." | Verb phrase ellipsis |
| "Who is going to present?" / "Maria is going to present." | "Maria **is [∅]**." | VP ellipsis |
| "I finished early. She finished early too." | "I finished early. She **did [∅]** too." | Auxiliary + ellipsis |
| "Are you ready?" / "Yes, I am ready." | "Yes, I **am [∅]**." | Be + ellipsis |

> **Infinitive Ellipsis with "to":**
> After modal verbs or in responses, the full infinitive can be replaced by "to" alone.
> - *"Did you call her?" — "I meant **to [call her]**."*
> - *"Are you going to the conference?" — "I'm hoping **to [go to the conference]**."*

---

#### Substitution — Examples and Forms

| Original (with repetition) | With Substitution | Substitute Used |
|---------------------------|------------------|-----------------|
| "The old report and the new report are both available." | "The old report and the new **one** are both available." | **one / ones** |
| "Did they finish the task?" / "Yes, they finished the task." | "Yes, they **did so**." | **do so** |
| "Will it rain?" / "I think it will rain." | "I think **so**." | **so** |
| "I don't think it will rain." | "I think **not**." | **not** |
| "She said the meeting was cancelled." / "If the meeting is cancelled…" | "If **that** is the case…" or "If **so**…" | **so / that** |

---

#### When to Use Which?

| Situation | Best Choice |
|-----------|-------------|
| Omitting a repeated verb phrase entirely | **Ellipsis** |
| Replacing a noun with a shorter reference | **Substitution (one/ones)** |
| Replacing a whole clause with a word | **Substitution (so/not/do so)** |
| Responding to questions naturally | **Both** (e.g., "I hope so", "I believe not") |

### 3. Reference Devices

Reference devices are words and phrases that **point to something already mentioned** (anaphoric reference) or that **signal what is about to be explained** (cataphoric reference). They create cohesion across sentences and paragraphs.

---

#### Reference Device Table

| Device | Function | Example in Context |
|--------|----------|-------------------|
| **This** | Points to the immediately preceding idea (anaphoric) or introduces what follows (cataphoric) | *The study was abandoned due to lack of funding. **This** decision drew significant criticism.* |
| **That** | Points back to a more distant or previously established idea | *Early civilisations used fire for warmth and cooking. **That** discovery transformed human history.* |
| **Such** | Refers back categorically — "of that type / of this kind" | *The policy created a hostile environment for investors. **Such** conditions are rarely conducive to growth.* |
| **The former** | Refers to the **first** of two previously mentioned items | *Between cohesion and coherence, **the former** relates to textual surface features.* |
| **The latter** | Refers to the **last** of two previously mentioned items | *Between talent and discipline, **the latter** is arguably more important for long-term success.* |

---

#### ⚠️ Rules for "the former" and "the latter"

- Only used when **exactly two items** have been mentioned.
- Always use **the** (definite article).
- Avoid overusing them — if they risk confusion, just repeat the noun.
- Common in formal written English; less frequent in everyday speech.

> ✅ *London and Paris are major European capitals. **The former** is known for its theatre; **the latter** for its cuisine.*
> ❌ Using "the former" after mentioning three or more items.

---

#### Model Paragraph with Reference Devices Marked

> *Two main approaches dominate the debate on language acquisition: the nativist theory and the empiricist view. **The former** holds that humans are born with an innate capacity for language. **The latter**, on the other hand, argues that language is learned entirely through exposure and experience. **Such** disagreements are common in cognitive science, where direct empirical evidence is difficult to obtain. **This** tension between innateness and environment continues to shape research in linguistics today, and **that** ongoing debate reflects a deeper question about the nature of the human mind.*

**Devices used:** the former *(nativist theory)* | the latter *(empiricist view)* | such *(disagreements like this one)* | this *(the tension just described)* | that *(the debate referred to)*

### 4. Hedging & Boosting

Hedging and boosting are tools for **calibrating the strength of a claim**. Academic and professional writers use these strategically — hedging to avoid overstatement, boosting to emphasise certainty or consensus.

---

#### Hedging — Softening Claims

> **Purpose:** To show that a statement is not absolute — it is based on evidence, interpretation, or probability. Hedging demonstrates intellectual humility and academic rigour.

| Hedging Expression | Type | Example |
|-------------------|------|---------|
| **tend to** | Semi-modal verb phrase | *Students **tend to** perform better when given regular feedback.* |
| **appear to** | Epistemic verb | *The results **appear to** confirm the original hypothesis.* |
| **seem to** | Epistemic verb | *The economy **seems to** be recovering at a steady pace.* |
| **be likely to** | Probability structure | *Remote work is **likely to** become the norm in many industries.* |
| **suggest that** | Reporting verb (data/evidence) | *The data **suggests that** early intervention is more effective.* |
| **indicate that** | Reporting verb (formal) | *The figures **indicate that** inequality has increased significantly.* |
| **might / may / could** | Modal verbs of possibility | *This **could** have long-term consequences for public health.* |
| **It is possible that** | Impersonal construction | ***It is possible that** the findings were influenced by sampling bias.* |

---

#### Boosting — Strengthening Claims

> **Purpose:** To convey certainty, emphasise consensus, or make a point more persuasive and forceful. Boosting is used when the speaker/writer has strong evidence or wants to stress a widely-held truth.

| Boosting Expression | Type | Example |
|--------------------|------|---------|
| **undoubtedly** | Adverb | *This is **undoubtedly** the most significant challenge of our time.* |
| **clearly** | Adverb | ***Clearly**, access to education has a direct impact on social mobility.* |
| **arguably** | Adverb (marks contested consensus) | *This is **arguably** the most influential book of the decade.* |
| **evidently** | Adverb | ***Evidently**, the previous approach was insufficient to address the problem.* |
| **it is widely accepted that** | Impersonal construction | ***It is widely accepted that** exercise has significant mental health benefits.* |
| **there is no question that** | Emphatic construction | ***There is no question that** climate change demands immediate action.* |
| **without doubt** | Adverbial phrase | ***Without doubt**, the reform transformed the education system.* |

---

#### ⚠️ Important: "Arguably" is NOT 100% certain

> **Arguably** is a boosting word, but it signals a **strong, defensible claim — not an undisputed fact**.
> It means: *"This can be argued, and I believe the argument is strong."*
> - ✅ *"Shakespeare is **arguably** the greatest writer in the English language."* (strong claim, widely held but not absolute)
> - ❌ Using *arguably* for clear facts: *"Paris is **arguably** the capital of France."*

---

#### Paragraph Transformation: Assertive → Hedged → Boosted

**Original (assertive / neutral):**
> *Remote work increases productivity. Employees perform better at home. This model will replace traditional office work.*

**With Hedging:**
> *Remote work **tends to** increase productivity **in many cases**. Employees **appear to** perform better at home, **at least** according to recent surveys. **It is possible that** this model **may** gradually replace traditional office work **to some extent**.*

**With Boosting:**
> ***Undoubtedly**, remote work increases productivity. **Clearly**, employees perform better at home. **There is no question that** this model will **ultimately** replace traditional office work.*

> 🔍 **Notice:** The hedged version qualifies and limits claims with evidence language. The boosted version asserts them forcefully. Both are more sophisticated than the flat original — which sounds neither academic nor persuasive.
