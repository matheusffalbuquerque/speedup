## Slide 2: What Is a Phrasal Verb? 🔎
### Verb + Particle = New Meaning

A **phrasal verb** is a verb combined with a small word, usually an adverb or preposition.  
This small word is called a **particle**.

**Basic structure:** verb + particle

| Verb | Particle | Phrasal verb | Meaning |
|---|---|---|---|
| give | up | **give up** | stop trying / stop doing something |
| look | after | **look after** | take care of |
| turn | on | **turn on** | start a machine, light, or device |
| find | out | **find out** | discover information |
| put | off | **put off** | postpone |

> 💡 Many phrasal verbs are very common in spoken English. They often sound more natural than a single formal verb.

## Slide 4: Literal vs Idiomatic Meaning 🧩
### The Meaning Is Not Always Obvious

Some phrasal verbs are easy to understand because the meaning is literal.  
Others are idiomatic, so the meaning is different from the individual words.

| Phrasal Verb | Type | Example | Meaning |
|---|---|---|---|
| **turn on** | literal | *Turn on the TV.* | start the TV |
| **turn off** | literal | *Turn off the oven.* | stop the oven |
| **look after** | idiomatic | *She looks after her grandparents.* | takes care of them |
| **give up** | idiomatic | *He gave up smoking.* | stopped smoking |
| **run into** | idiomatic | *We ran into our teacher at the mall.* | met by chance |
| **look forward to** | idiomatic | *I'm looking forward to seeing you.* | feel excited about |

> 💡 A good strategy is to learn phrasal verbs in full sentences, not as isolated word pairs.

## Slide 5: Separable Phrasal Verbs ✂️
### When the Object Can Go in the Middle

Some phrasal verbs are **separable**.  
This means the object can go after the phrasal verb or between the verb and the particle.

**Example: turn off**

| Word Order | Example |
|---|---|
| verb + particle + object | *Please turn off the computer.* |
| verb + object + particle | *Please turn the computer off.* |

Both forms are correct when the object is a noun.

**Important rule with pronouns:**

| ❌ Incorrect | ✅ Correct |
|---|---|
| *Please turn off it.* | *Please turn it off.* |
| *Can you turn on them?* | *Can you turn them on?* |

> 💡 With pronouns like **it**, **them**, **him**, and **her**, the pronoun must go in the middle.

---

## Slide 6: More Separable Phrasal Verbs 🔁
### Common Word Order Patterns

| Phrasal Verb | Noun Object | Pronoun Object |
|---|---|---|
| **turn on** | *Turn on the air conditioner.* / *Turn the air conditioner on.* | *Turn it on.* |
| **turn off** | *Turn off the lights.* / *Turn the lights off.* | *Turn them off.* |
| **put off** | *They put off the trip.* / *They put the trip off.* | *They put it off.* |
| **find out** | *I found out the truth.* / *I found the truth out.* | *I found it out.* |

**Natural usage note:**

For longer noun objects, English speakers often keep the object after the full phrasal verb:

- *They put off the meeting with the international clients.* ✅
- *They put the meeting with the international clients off.* ✅ possible, but heavier

## Slide 7: Inseparable Phrasal Verbs 🔒
### When the Words Stay Together

Some phrasal verbs are **inseparable**.  
This means the object always comes after the complete phrasal verb.

| Phrasal Verb | Correct Example | Incorrect Example |
|---|---|---|
| **look after** | *She looks after her nephew.* | *She looks her nephew after.* |
| **run into** | *I ran into my neighbor.* | *I ran my neighbor into.* |
| **get along with** | *They get along with each other.* | *They get each other along with.* |
| **look forward to** | *I'm looking forward to the event.* | *I'm looking the event forward to.* |

> 💡 If a phrasal verb has two particles, like **look forward to** or **get along with**, it is usually inseparable.

## Slide 8: "Look Forward To" + Noun or -ING ⏳
### A Very Common Advanced Pattern

**Look forward to** means to feel happy or excited about something in the future.

The word **to** in this expression is a preposition, not an infinitive marker.  
That is why the next verb uses **-ing**.

| Structure | Example |
|---|---|
| look forward to + noun | *I'm looking forward to the trip.* |
| look forward to + -ing verb | *I'm looking forward to traveling.* |
| look forward to + possessive + -ing | *We're looking forward to your visiting us.* |

**Common mistake:**

| ❌ Incorrect | ✅ Correct |
|---|---|
| *I'm looking forward to see you.* | *I'm looking forward to seeing you.* |
| *She looks forward to start her new job.* | *She looks forward to starting her new job.* |

## Slide 9: Phrasal Verbs with Multiple Meanings 🔄
### Context Changes Everything

Many phrasal verbs have more than one meaning.  
The context tells you which meaning is being used.

| Phrasal Verb | Meaning 1 | Example | Meaning 2 | Example |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **give up** | stop trying | *Don't give up. You can do it.* | stop a habit | *He gave up sugar last year.* |
| **put off** | postpone | *We put off the meeting.* | discourage / make someone dislike | *His rude attitude put me off.* |
| **turn on** | start a device | *Turn on the laptop.* | suddenly attack / change against | *The crowd turned on the speaker.* |
| **turn off** | stop a device | *Turn off the TV.* | make someone lose interest | *The bad smell turned me off.* |
| **run into** | meet by chance | *I ran into Maria downtown.* | experience a problem | *We ran into some technical issues.* |

> 💡 When the meaning is not clear, look at the object and the situation around the phrasal verb.

## Slide 11: Formal vs Informal Alternatives 🎩👕
### Choosing the Right Register

Phrasal verbs are very common in everyday English.  
In formal writing, a one-word verb may sometimes sound more appropriate.

| Everyday English | More Formal Alternative | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **give up** | quit / abandon | *He abandoned the project after several delays.* |
| **look after** | take care of / supervise | *The nurse takes care of the patients.* |
| **turn on** | activate / start | *Please activate the system.* |
| **turn off** | deactivate / stop | *The technician deactivated the alarm.* |
| **find out** | discover / determine | *We need to determine the cause of the problem.* |
| **put off** | postpone | *The meeting was postponed.* |
| **get along with** | have a good relationship with | *She has a good relationship with her colleagues.* |
| **run into** | encounter | *We encountered some difficulties.* |

> 💡 Phrasal verbs are not "wrong" in formal English, but a more formal synonym can sound clearer in reports, emails, and presentations.

## Slide 12: Common Mistakes 🚫
### Errors Brazilian Students Often Make

| ❌ Incorrect | ✅ Correct | Why? |
|---|---|---|
| *I'm looking forward to see you.* | *I'm looking forward to seeing you.* | After **look forward to**, use **-ing** |
| *Turn off it, please.* | *Turn it off, please.* | Pronouns go between verb and particle |
| *She looks her brother after.* | *She looks after her brother.* | **Look after** is inseparable |
| *I found out about that she left.* | *I found out that she left.* | Use **find out that + clause** |
| *I gave up to smoke.* | *I gave up smoking.* | After **give up**, use noun or **-ing** |
| *We put off to travel.* | *We put off traveling.* | After **put off**, use noun or **-ing** |
| *I get along good with them.* | *I get along well with them.* | Use **well** for how you get along |
| *I ran into with my friend.* | *I ran into my friend.* | No extra **with** after **run into** |

## Slide 13: Putting It All Together 🌱
### A Natural Conversation with Phrasal Verbs

**Context:** Two friends talking about plans, family, and work.

---

> **Lia:** *Are you still coming to dinner tonight?*  
> **Marco:** *I hope so, but I might be late. I need to look after my little cousin for an hour.*  
> **Lia:** *No problem. We can put off dinner until eight.*  
> **Marco:** *Thanks. I'm really looking forward to seeing everyone.*  
> **Lia:** *Me too. By the way, I ran into Paula yesterday.*  
> **Marco:** *Really? I haven't seen her in years. Did you find out where she's working now?*  
> **Lia:** *Yes, she works downtown. She said she gets along really well with her new team.*  
> **Marco:** *That's great. I almost gave up trying to contact her because she changed her number.*  
> **Lia:** *I'll send it to you later. And don't forget to turn off the lights before you leave home.*  

---

**Notice how the conversation uses:**
- ✅ **look after** for taking care of someone
- ✅ **put off** for postponing a plan
- ✅ **look forward to + -ing** for future excitement
- ✅ **run into** for meeting someone by chance
- ✅ **find out** for discovering information
- ✅ **get along with** for relationships
- ✅ **give up + -ing** for stopping an effort
- ✅ **turn off + object** for devices and lights
